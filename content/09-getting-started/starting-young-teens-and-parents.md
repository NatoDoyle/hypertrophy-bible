# Starting Young: Teens and Parents

> **TL;DR** — Resistance training (working muscles against a load — weights, machines, or bodyweight) is safe and beneficial for teenagers when it's supervised and done with good technique. It does **not** stunt growth — that's an old myth with no evidence behind it. For young lifters the priority is learning to move well and adding load gradually, not chasing the heaviest possible weight. Good coaching or supervision is the single biggest factor in doing this safely and getting real results.

**Quick recommendations**
- **Learn technique first, add weight second.** Master the movement light before loading it up. **[Grade D]**
- **Supervise young lifters** — a coach, knowledgeable parent, or qualified trainer, especially early on. **[Grade D]**
- **Progress gradually.** Small, steady load increases beat maximal ("how much can I lift once?") attempts. **[Grade D]**
- **Rest strength training does not stunt growth** — there's no evidence it harms development when done properly. **[Grade C]**
- Start with **2–3 sessions a week**, plenty of movement variety, and full recovery between sessions. **[Grade D]**

## Resistance training is safe for youth

Major sports-medicine and paediatric bodies agree: supervised, well-coached resistance training is safe and healthy for children and adolescents. It builds strength, supports bone health, improves coordination and athletic performance, and can boost confidence and body image. Injury rates for properly supervised youth strength training are low — lower than in many common sports like football or gymnastics.

The key words are *supervised* and *well-coached*. The injuries that do happen in this age group tend to come from unsupervised ego-lifting: maxing out, using bad technique, dropping weights, or messing around near equipment. Remove those, and you remove most of the risk.

## The "stunts growth" myth

This is the worry most parents raise, and it is not supported by the evidence. The idea comes from a fear of injuring the *growth plates* — areas of developing cartilage near the ends of long bones. But there's no good evidence that appropriate, supervised resistance training damages growth plates or reduces final adult height. Growth-plate injuries in youth overwhelmingly come from acute trauma in sports and accidents, not from controlled strength training.

So a teenager who lifts sensibly, with supervision and good form, is not trading away height or development. If anything, they're building bone density, coordination, and lifelong healthy habits.

## Emphasise technique and gradual loading

For a young lifter, the goal is *skill and consistency*, not a one-rep max. Build the program around movement quality:

- **Technique before load.** Groove each exercise with a light weight — or just bodyweight — until it looks clean and controlled. Load is a reward for good movement, not a substitute for it.
- **Gradual progression.** Add small amounts over weeks. There is no rush; the young athlete has years ahead of them.
- **Avoid maximal singles.** Testing "how much can I lift once?" is unnecessary and where technique breaks down. Working in moderate rep ranges (say 6–15 reps) with reps left in reserve is both safer and highly effective.
- **Variety.** A mix of pushing, pulling, squatting, hinging, and carrying builds a well-rounded athlete and keeps it fun.
- **Recovery.** Teenagers train hard in many settings — school sport, clubs, PE. Factor that total load in and make sure there's real rest.

## The role of coaching and supervision

Good supervision does three jobs at once: it keeps the young lifter safe, it teaches technique that lasts a lifetime, and it builds confidence through encouragement rather than intimidation. That supervision can come from a qualified strength coach, a knowledgeable PE teacher or club coach, or an informed parent who has learned the movements themselves.

For parents new to this: you don't need to be an expert, but do make sure *someone* competent is guiding the early sessions, the environment is calm rather than competitive, and the emphasis stays on doing things well rather than lifting the most. That sets a young person up for decades of safe, rewarding training.

## Common mistakes

- **Ego-lifting and maxing out.** Heavy singles with poor form are where youth injuries happen.
- **No supervision early on.** The learning phase is exactly when guidance matters most.
- **Believing the growth myth** and avoiding training that would actually benefit a young person.
- **Copying advanced routines online** meant for adult or enhanced lifters.
- **Ignoring total load** from school sport and clubs, leaving no room to recover.
