# Choosing and Joining a Gym

> **TL;DR** — Almost any gym with a barbell, a rack, some dumbbells, an adjustable bench, and a cable machine can run the programs in this Bible. Choose on four things: the equipment checklist below, how far it is, how crowded it gets **at the hours you'll actually train**, and a contract you can get out of. Tour in person on a day pass before you sign anything.

**Quick recommendations**
- Tour in person on a **day or trial pass**, at the time of day you'd normally train. **[Grade D]**
- Confirm the **equipment checklist** below before you join. **[Grade D]**
- Prefer **month-to-month** over a long contract until you know the habit will stick. **[Grade D]**
- Judge crowding at your **real** training hours, not at whatever quiet moment you happened to tour. **[Grade D]**

## Types of gyms, and the trade-offs for a beginner
- **Big-box commercial** (large full-service chains): lots of machines, dumbbells, and cables; a reliable all-rounder for hypertrophy. Can get packed at peak times.
- **Budget 24-hour chains:** cheap and always open, usually with solid machines and dumbbells. Sometimes short on squat racks or barbells — verify before assuming.
- **Boutique / studio:** great atmosphere and classes, but often lacks the free weights and racks you need for progressive barbell work. Fine as a supplement, weak as your only gym.
- **CrossFit boxes:** barbells, racks, and community in abundance, but the class-based clock can fight a hypertrophy template. Check for open-gym hours.
- **Hardcore / powerlifting specialist:** excellent free weights and racks, sometimes fewer machines and cables. Superb equipment if the intense vibe doesn't put you off.
- **University / workplace / hotel:** convenient and cheap or free, but equipment varies enormously — check it against the list below.
- **Home:** covered in its own page (linked at the bottom).

## How to tour a gym
Get a **day or trial pass** and go at the hour you'd actually train. While you're there, check: how many squat racks there are and whether they're free, the heaviest dumbbells available, the condition of the cable stack and machines, general cleanliness, and — most importantly — how busy it really is.

## Equipment checklist: can this gym run the KB's programs?
- At least one **barbell + squat/power rack** (two or more is much better for busy hours).
- An **adjustable bench** (flat and incline).
- **Dumbbells** up to a genuinely useful weight — at least ~40 kg / 90 lb, ideally heavier, in reasonable increments.
- A **cable stack** (an adjustable pulley).
- A few **key machines** — a leg press or hack squat, a lat pulldown, a leg curl, a chest press. These are strong nice-to-haves, not deal-breakers.

If a gym has the first four, it can run essentially every template here. The machines just add variety and convenience.

## Month-to-month vs contract, and cancellation traps
A rolling **month-to-month** membership costs a little more per month but lets you leave freely — worth it until the habit is established. If you sign a longer contract, read the fine print for the common traps: **automatic renewal**, a **notice period** (often 30–60 days before you can cancel), **cancellation fees**, and rules for **freezing** your membership. Don't lock into twelve months before your first workout.

## Crowding and peak hours
Peak times are early morning and roughly **4–8pm on weekdays**. If those are the only hours you can train, crowding matters a lot: a single-rack gym means waiting, and a contested floor makes it hard to run your exercises in order or superset. A gym with **duplicate racks and benches** is worth paying a bit more for if you're a peak-hours trainee.

## Common mistakes
- Signing a 12-month contract before you've done a single workout there.
- Touring at 11am, then meeting the 6pm crush after you've joined.
- Choosing a studio or class gym that can't support progressive barbell work.
- Skipping the rack count and finding one rack shared by thirty people.
- Ignoring the cancellation terms until you're trying to leave.
