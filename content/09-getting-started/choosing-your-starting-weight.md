# Choosing Your Starting Weight

> **TL;DR** — Every program tells you to lift "your working weight," but nobody tells you how to find it the first time. The answer: start deliberately light, do a set, notice how hard it felt, and add a little more each set until the last rep of your target reps feels genuinely hard but not impossible — about **2–3 RIR** (reps in reserve: how many more reps you could have done before failing). You will figure this out within one or two sessions, so err on the light side. Guessing a bit low costs you nothing; guessing high risks bad form or a tweak.

**Quick recommendations**
- For a new exercise, start with **just the empty bar, the lightest dumbbells, or the lowest machine pin** and do your target reps. **[Grade D]**
- Judge how hard that set felt in terms of **RIR** — if you had 5+ reps left in the tank, it was too light; add weight. **[Grade D]**
- Add weight in **small jumps** each set (about 2.5–5 kg / 5–10 lb on most lifts) until the last rep is hard but clean, roughly **2–3 RIR**. **[Grade D]**
- **Write down the weight and reps that landed at 2–3 RIR.** That is your working weight for next time. **[Grade D]**
- When in doubt, **go lighter.** You lose nothing by calibrating up over a couple of sessions. **[Grade C]**

## The chicken-and-egg problem

Programs assume you already know what weight to put on the bar. But if this is your first time doing an exercise, you have no idea. That is completely normal, and there is a simple way to solve it: instead of trying to *predict* the right weight, you **discover** it by doing a few sets and paying attention.

## What "the right weight" actually means

For building muscle, you want each working set to end **close to failure** but not at it — a hard set, not a max effort. We measure that closeness with **RIR (reps in reserve)**: the number of reps you could still have done if you'd kept going. A weight where the last rep of your target reps leaves you with about **2–3 RIR** is a great place to start. It is hard enough to matter and easy enough that your form holds and you stay safe. (See [proximity to failure](../01-training-variables/proximity-to-failure.md) for why this range works.)

## The ramp-up method, step by step

Say your program says **3 sets of 10** on the leg press.

1. **Feeler set.** Load something you're sure is easy — for a machine, the lightest one or two plates. Do 10 reps. Ask yourself: *how many more could I have done?*
2. **Read the RIR.** If the answer is "loads, maybe 8+," it was too light. If it was "maybe 4–5," you're getting close.
3. **Add a small amount** — one plate, or one pin down, or 2.5–5 kg. Do another set of 10. Re-read the RIR.
4. **Repeat** until 10 reps ends at about **2–3 RIR**. That is your working weight today.
5. **Record it.** Weight and reps, in your phone or a notebook. Next session you start there instead of from scratch.

A couple of extra ramp-up sets on your first day is not wasted effort — it *is* the workout for finding your numbers. (These light sets don't count as your hard working sets; see [warm-ups](../03-programming/warm-up.md).)

## How fast you'll calibrate

Faster than you think. Most people nail a usable working weight for each exercise within **one or two sessions**. Different exercises will land at very different weights — you might leg-press far more than you can shoulder-press — and that is normal. From then on, you nudge the weight up gradually as you get stronger; the standard beginner method is **double progression** (add reps until you hit the top of your range, then add a little weight and drop back down). That's covered in [periodization and progression](../03-programming/periodization-and-progression.md).

## Common mistakes

- **Starting too heavy to feel "serious."** Ego-loading on day one is how form breaks and things get tweaked. Nobody is judging your first weight.
- **Chasing failure on the feeler sets.** You're calibrating, not testing your max. Stop each ramp set with reps in reserve.
- **Not writing anything down.** If you don't record your numbers, you re-solve this puzzle every session. Log it once and move on.
- **Expecting the same weight across exercises.** Big compound lifts and small isolation lifts live at totally different loads. Calibrate each one on its own.
- **Adding weight in giant jumps.** Doubling the load between sets overshoots. Small steps land you on target faster.
