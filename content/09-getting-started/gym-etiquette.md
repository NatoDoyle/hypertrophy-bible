# Gym Etiquette

> **TL;DR** — Gym etiquette is mostly common courtesy with one shared goal: keep the space usable and safe for everyone in it. If you put your weights back, wipe up your sweat, share equipment when someone's waiting, and give people room, you already fit in. Nobody expects you to know the rest on day one, and the regulars care far less about you than your nerves are telling you.

**Quick recommendations**
- Return **every** weight — dumbbells, plates, the bar — the moment you finish with it. **[Grade D]**
- Carry a small towel and wipe down any bench, pad, or handle you sweated on. **[Grade D]**
- When someone's waiting, offer to **"work in"** (take turns) and ask to share politely. **[Grade D]**
- Keep your bag, your phone breaks, and yourself out of walkways and out of people's lifts. **[Grade D]**
- Angle your camera away from strangers before filming, and check whether your gym bans loose chalk. **[Grade D]**

None of this is gatekeeping. It's just the set of small habits that stop a busy room full of strangers from getting in each other's way.

## Re-rack and return everything
**Why:** the next person needs that weight, and loose plates on the floor are a genuine trip hazard. Strip the barbell when you're done, return dumbbells to the correct numbered slot, and put plates back on the tree sorted by size. A bar left loaded also makes the next lifter guess and unload before they can even start.

## Wipe down what you touch
**Why:** hygiene. Sweat carries bacteria and nobody wants to lie in yours. Bring a small towel, and use the spray or wipes most gyms provide on any bench or pad after you use it. This is the single easiest way to be a good gym citizen.

## Sharing and "working in"
**Working in** means taking turns on one station during your rest periods so it isn't tied up. If someone's hovering, say *"How many sets do you have left?"* or *"Mind if I work in?"* — both are completely normal. When you share, reset the weight to what they need between your turns. On long rests, letting someone jump in costs you nothing.

## Don't hog or camp
**Why:** a bench or machine held hostage by someone scrolling their phone blocks everyone behind them. Keep rest periods reasonable, don't set up a base camp of towel-water-phone on a machine between sets, and take your phone breaks off to the side so the station stays free.

## Space and walkways
Stash your bag in a locker or against a wall, never in an aisle. Don't cut right in front of someone mid-set — especially near mirrors, where people are checking their form. Give lifters, particularly anyone under a heavy barbell, a wide, predictable path.

## Phones, filming, and chalk
Filming your own sets is normal now, but other people may be in the frame. Angle the camera toward yourself, and if someone's clearly in shot, just ask. Many gyms ban **loose chalk** (the powdered grip aid) because of the mess and dust; liquid chalk is usually fine, but check the posted rules first.

## Don't slam weights or coach strangers
Lowering a weight under control is part of the lift; **dropping** loaded dumbbells or barbells when you don't need to is loud, damages equipment, and startles people. (Some gyms with platforms and bumper plates expect a controlled drop on heavy deadlifts — learn what yours allows.) And unless someone is in immediate danger, **don't** correct a stranger's form. Unsolicited coaching almost always lands as condescending, however kindly you mean it.

## Locker room and hygiene note
The same courtesy applies out of sight. Don't spread your gear across a shared bench, don't leave the shower or sink a mess, and a little deodorant and a clean kit go a long way in a warm, shared room. Treat the locker room like the training floor: leave it as you'd want to find it.

## Common mistakes
- Leaving plates on the bar or dumbbells on the floor for the next person to clear.
- Sitting on a machine scrolling between sets while others wait.
- Filming with strangers in frame without a second thought.
- Using loose chalk where it's banned.
- "Helping" a lifter who never asked.
