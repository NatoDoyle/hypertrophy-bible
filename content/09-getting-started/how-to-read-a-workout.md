# How to Read a Workout

> **TL;DR** — A written workout looks like code at first (`3x8 @ 2 RIR`), but it's just shorthand for "how many sets, how many reps, and how hard." Once you learn a handful of symbols you can read any program. This page decodes the notation and walks one full workout line from the page to what you actually do on the gym floor.

**Quick recommendations**
- Read the format as **sets × reps @ effort** — e.g. `3x8 @ 2 RIR` = 3 sets of 8 reps, stopping with 2 reps left in you. **[Grade D]**
- When you see a **rep range** like `3x8-12`, aim for the top number, and add weight once you hit it on every set (that's "double progression"). **[Grade D]**
- Treat the **@ RIR** part as the most important number — it controls how hard you push, which drives most of the growth. **[Grade C]**
- Do your **warm-up sets first**; they're assumed, not written. **[Grade D]**

## The core format: sets × reps @ effort

Almost every prescription follows one pattern:

> **sets `x` reps `@` effort**

- **Sets come first, reps second.** `3x8` means **3 sets of 8 reps** — three rounds of 8. (It never means 8 sets of 3. If someone means that, they write `8x3`.)
- **`@` gives the effort or the load.** It's read as "at." It may be an RIR target (`@ 2 RIR`), an RPE (`@ RPE 8`), or a weight/percentage (`@ 80 kg`, `@ 75%`).

So `3x8 @ 2 RIR` = **three sets of eight reps, each set stopped when you have about two reps left in reserve** (RIR = Reps In Reserve; the reps you could still do but don't).

## Rep ranges and "double progression"

A range like **`3x8-12`** means 3 sets, and each set should land **somewhere between 8 and 12 reps**. You don't pick one number forever — you use it to progress:

1. Pick a weight you can do for at least the **bottom** of the range (8).
2. Each session, try to add reps toward the **top** (12).
3. Once you hit the **top of the range on all your sets**, add a little weight next time and start again nearer the bottom.

That "add reps, then add weight" cycle is called **double progression**, and it's the simplest way for a beginner to keep progressively overloading.

## Tempo notation: four numbers

Sometimes you'll see something like **`3-0-1-0`** next to an exercise. These are **seconds per phase of one rep**, always in this order:

- **1st number — lowering** (the "down" or eccentric phase): 3 seconds.
- **2nd number — pause at the bottom**: 0 seconds (no pause).
- **3rd number — lifting** (the "up" or concentric phase): 1 second.
- **4th number — pause at the top**: 0 seconds.

So `3-0-1-0` on a squat = lower for 3 seconds, no pause, stand up in 1 second, no pause. If no tempo is written, use a **controlled lower (~1–2 s) and a smooth lift** — that's the sensible default. **[Grade D]**

## Supersets: the A1 / A2 notation

Letters group exercises that share a slot. **`A1` and `A2`** means do a set of A1, then straight into a set of A2, *then* rest — that pairing is a **superset**. `B1/B2` is the next pair, and a lone `C` is a normal standalone exercise. Pairing non-competing muscles (e.g. a back exercise with a chest exercise) saves time without hurting either lift much.

## Rest, warm-ups, and working sets

- **Rest between sets** is often written as `rest 2–3 min`. If it isn't stated, rest until your breathing settles and you feel ready — roughly **2–3 minutes for hard compounds, 1–2 for isolation**. **[Grade C]**
- **Warm-up sets are assumed, not printed.** A written `3x8` means 3 *working* sets; you still do your lighter ramp-up sets first, and they don't count toward that number.

## A fully worked example

Here's a real line as it would appear in a program:

> **A1. Barbell Bench Press — 3x8-12 @ 2 RIR, 3-0-1-0, rest 2 min**
> **A2. Dumbbell Row — 3x10-15 @ 2 RIR, rest 2 min**

On the gym floor, that means:

1. **Warm up** the bench with a few ascending lighter sets (not counted).
2. **Bench working set 1:** pick a weight you can press for 8–12 reps; lower for 3 seconds, press up in 1; stop with ~2 reps left.
3. **Straight into Dumbbell Row** (the A2) for 10–15 reps, again leaving ~2 in reserve.
4. **Now rest ~2 minutes.** That completes one round of the A pair.
5. **Repeat** for a total of 3 rounds.
6. **Progression:** when you get 12 reps on all 3 bench sets, add weight next session and drop back toward 8.

Read it once slowly and it stops looking like code.

## Common mistakes

- **Reading `3x8` as sets-then-reps backwards.** Sets always come first. `3x8` is 3 sets of 8, not 8 of 3.
- **Ignoring the `@ RIR` and just chasing the rep number.** Grinding to failure on everything, or stopping far short, both miss the target — the effort cue matters as much as the reps.
- **Counting warm-up sets as working sets.** Only the prescribed sets count; warm-ups are extra.
- **Treating a rep range as a fixed number.** `8-12` is a runway for progression, not a demand for exactly 8.
- **Adding weight the moment you hit the top rep once.** Wait until you hit the top of the range on *all* sets before loading up.
- **Rushing supersets with no plan.** A1/A2 means paired sets then rest — not sprinting through everything with no recovery.
