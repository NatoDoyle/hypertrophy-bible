# Goal Setting

> **TL;DR** — Good goals keep you training when motivation dips. The trick is to lead with **process goals** — things you control, like "train 3 times this week" or "add a rep to my rows" — rather than only chasing **outcome goals** like "lose 10 kg" that depend on biology and time you can't rush. Keep an outcome in mind for direction, but score yourself on the actions, give changes realistic timelines (think months, not weeks), and revisit your goals every couple of months as you learn what actually fits your life.

**Quick recommendations**
- **Set 1–2 process goals you fully control** (sessions per week, reps added, protein hit). Make these your main scoreboard. **[Grade D]**
- Keep an outcome goal for **direction only**, and give it a **realistic timeline** — muscle and fat change over months. **[Grade D]**
- Make goals **specific and measurable**: "train 3×/week for 8 weeks", not "get fitter". **[Grade D]**
- **Revisit goals every 8–12 weeks.** Adjust what isn't working instead of quitting. **[Grade D]**
- Expect roughly **0.5–1 kg (1–2 lb) of muscle per month** as a realistic year-one ceiling for a natural beginner (and less as you advance) — plan around that, not the internet's promises. **[Grade C]**

## Outcome goals vs process goals

An **outcome goal** is a result you want: lose 10 kg, add 5 cm to your arms, bench your bodyweight. A **process goal** is an action you take: train three times a week, hit your protein target, add a rep to an exercise when you can.

Outcome goals are useful for pointing you in a direction, but they make a poor day-to-day scoreboard because you don't fully control them. Your bodyweight can bounce around 1–2 kg overnight from water and food. Muscle takes months to show. If your motivation depends on the scale moving *today*, you'll be discouraged constantly by noise that has nothing to do with your effort.

Process goals fix this. You *completely control* whether you showed up and trained. Every session you complete is a guaranteed win you can bank, regardless of what the mirror or scale says that morning. And here's the quiet truth: **the process goals are what actually produce the outcomes.** Nail "train consistently, progress gradually, eat enough protein" for long enough, and the outcome takes care of itself.

## Why process goals win

- **You control them,** so they're always achievable through effort alone.
- **They give frequent wins,** which keeps motivation up through the slow stretches where the outcome hasn't visibly moved yet.
- **They're what drives results** — showing up and progressing *is* the mechanism, so measuring the action measures the thing that matters.
- **They survive bad days.** A poor-sleep, low-energy session you still completed is a success by a process goal and a "failure" only by an impatient outcome goal.

A simple structure: pick one training process goal ("3 sessions a week"), one progression goal ("add a rep or a little weight when I can"), and optionally one nutrition goal ("protein target most days"). That's a scoreboard you can win every single week.

## Realistic timelines

Impatience quietly kills more beginners' progress than anything else, because the expectations were never realistic to begin with. Some honest anchors:

- **Visible muscle:** noticeable change usually takes **2–4 months** of consistent training, and meaningful change **6–12 months**.
- **Muscle gain rate:** a natural beginner might gain around **0.5–1 kg (1–2 lb) of muscle per month** at best in the first year — and it slows as you get more advanced.
- **Strength:** often moves faster and earlier than size, which is why tracking your lifts is so motivating.
- **Fat loss:** a sustainable rate is roughly **0.5–1% of bodyweight per week**; faster tends to cost you muscle and adherence.

Set your timelines against these, not against transformation ads. Slow and steady genuinely is how it works.

## Revisiting goals

Goals are not set in stone — they're a hypothesis about what fits your life, and you'll get better data every week. Every **8–12 weeks**, sit down and ask:

- Did I actually hit my process goals? If not, was the goal too ambitious for my current schedule?
- Is the outcome moving in the right direction over the whole period (ignoring day-to-day noise)?
- Has my life changed — new job, injury, more or less time — in a way that means the plan should change too?

Then adjust. Lowering a goal to something you'll actually do is a smart course-correction, not a defeat. The only real failure is quitting; almost everything else is just editing the plan.

## Common mistakes

- **Only setting outcome goals,** then losing heart when they don't move on your schedule.
- **Vague goals** like "get in shape" with nothing measurable to aim at.
- **Unrealistic timelines** borrowed from marketing rather than physiology.
- **Never revisiting goals,** so a plan that stopped fitting your life quietly gets abandoned.
- **Treating a missed target as total failure** instead of information to adjust with.
