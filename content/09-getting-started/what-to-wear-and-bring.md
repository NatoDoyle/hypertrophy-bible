# What to Wear and Bring

> **TL;DR** — You need far less than the internet suggests. Comfortable clothes you can move and sweat in, plus a stable, flat-soled shoe, cover almost everything. Bring water, a small towel, and a padlock. Everything else — belts, straps, wraps, sleeves — is optional, has a narrow job, and can wait until you actually hit the problem it solves.

**Quick recommendations**
- Wear clothing that **moves and breathes**; nothing that restricts a full deep squat or an overhead reach. **[Grade D]**
- Lift in a **flat, firm-soled shoe** (or barefoot-style) for squats and deadlifts — not soft running shoes. **[Grade D]**
- Bring the basics: **water, a small towel, a padlock, headphones.** **[Grade D]**
- Treat a **belt, straps, wraps, and sleeves as optional add-ons**, not starting gear — none of them are required to begin. **[Grade D]**

## Clothing
Aim for freedom of movement and breathability. A t-shirt or tank with shorts or joggers is plenty, as long as you can drop into a full squat and reach overhead without anything pinching or riding up. Bring a layer for cold gyms. That's genuinely the whole clothing decision.

## The shoe question
This is the one piece of gear worth getting right early, and it costs nothing extra.

Under a heavy squat or deadlift, a soft, squishy **running-shoe** sole compresses unevenly — it's like lifting on a mattress. That wobble wastes force and makes your base unstable. A **flat, firm sole** — cheap canvas shoes, flat trainers, barefoot-style shoes, or dedicated lifting shoes — gives you a solid platform to push from.

**Lifting shoes** (with a raised, hard heel) are a genuine nice-to-have, not a requirement: the raised heel helps some people reach squat depth if they have limited ankle mobility. Cushioned running shoes are perfectly good for the treadmill or cardio — just not for heavy lifting.

## What to bring: the minimum viable kit
- **Water bottle.**
- **Small towel** — for wiping down equipment (that's etiquette) and yourself.
- **Padlock** for the locker; check whether your gym needs a specific type.
- **Headphones**, if music helps you focus.
- Optional: a snack or shaker if you train long or on an empty stomach.

## Optional gear (none required to start)
Each of these solves one specific problem. Add it **when** that problem shows up, not before.

| Gear | What it does | When it helps |
|------|--------------|---------------|
| **Lifting belt** | Gives your braced core something to push against | Heavy squats/deadlifts near failure — after you've learned to brace without one |
| **Lifting straps** | Fixes your grip to the bar | Heavy pulls and rows where your grip fails before the target muscle does |
| **Wrist wraps** | Support the wrist joint | Heavy pressing and overhead work |
| **Knee sleeves** | Warmth and light compression | Comfort more than performance; some people just like them |

The honest summary: **none of these build muscle.** They let you keep loading a lift when a weak link — grip, wrist, comfort — gets in the way of training the muscle you're actually there for. Buy them one at a time, if and when you meet the specific problem each one addresses.

## Common mistakes
- Buying a belt and straps before your very first session, when you don't yet know how to brace or grip hard.
- Squatting or deadlifting heavy in soft, cushioned running shoes.
- Wearing clothing that blocks squat depth or an overhead reach.
- Forgetting a padlock and leaving valuables out on the floor.
