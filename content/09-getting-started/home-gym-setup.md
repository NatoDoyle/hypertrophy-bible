# Home Gym Setup

> **TL;DR** — You can run the programs in this Bible at home with surprisingly little. The true minimum is a pair of **adjustable dumbbells** (or a **barbell + plates + a rack with safety arms + an adjustable bench**). Everything beyond that just adds convenience and exercise variety. Start small, and substitute smartly rather than chasing a full commercial gym in your garage.

**Quick recommendations**
- **Minimum viable home gym:** adjustable dumbbells + an adjustable bench, **or** a barbell + plates + a rack with safeties + a bench. **[Grade D]**
- If you barbell-train alone, use a **rack with adjustable safety arms** so you can push near failure without a spotter. **[Grade D]**
- **Substitute freely** across equipment — match the movement pattern and the target muscle, not the exact exercise name. **[Grade D]**

## The minimum viable kit
There are two sensible starting points, and either one is enough to train every major muscle.

1. **Adjustable dumbbells + an adjustable bench.** This covers presses, rows, curls, extensions, lunges, and hip-hinge (Romanian deadlift) patterns. It's compact, relatively affordable, and safe — if a rep fails, you just set or drop the dumbbells beside you.
2. **Barbell + plates + a power rack with safeties + an adjustable bench.** This gives a much higher ceiling for heavy squats, presses, rows, and deadlifts. The **safeties** (also called spotter arms or safety pins) are the key part: they catch the bar if you fail a rep, which lets you train hard and close to failure with no training partner in the room.

Pick based on space, budget, and how heavy you expect to get. Dumbbells are the easier, safer start; a barbell rack is the higher long-term ceiling.

## Progressively better setups
Add equipment in the order that fills the biggest gap in your program, not by whatever's on sale:
- **First additions:** a pull-up bar (for a vertical pull), resistance bands, and heavier adjustable dumbbells.
- **Next:** a cable pulley or functional trainer, which unlocks a huge range of isolation work.
- **Later luxuries:** a leg curl/extension attachment, a lat pulldown, a dip station, a landmine attachment.

Most home setups are missing a **vertical pull** and dedicated **hamstring** work first — solve those before buying a fifth pressing option.

## Substituting when equipment is limited
The principle: **match the movement pattern and the target muscle**, and the exact exercise barely matters.

| You don't have... | Use instead |
|-------------------|-------------|
| Leg press / hack squat | Goblet squat, Bulgarian split squat with dumbbells |
| Lat pulldown | Pull-ups, banded pulldowns, dumbbell rows |
| Leg curl | Romanian deadlift, Nordic curl, sliding leg curl |
| Cable stack | Resistance bands or dumbbells |
| Barbell | The dumbbell version of the same pattern |

One adjustment matters with lighter home loads: if you can't load a lift heavily, **push closer to failure and add reps or sets** to keep the muscle-building stimulus where it needs to be. Light weight taken well short of failure is where home training quietly stops working.

## Common mistakes
- Buying a barbell with **no rack or safeties** — you can't fail a rep safely, so you'll never push hard alone.
- Spending on fancy machines before you've covered the basic movement patterns.
- Copying a gym program movement-for-movement instead of substituting by pattern.
- Loads too light with no plan to progress, so every set drifts far from failure.
