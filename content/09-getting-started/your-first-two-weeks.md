# Your First Two Weeks

> **TL;DR** — For roughly your first two weeks, the goal is **not** to lift as heavy as possible. It's to **learn the movements** and **build the habit** of showing up. Keep every set well short of failure — about **3–4 RIR** (reps in reserve: how many more reps you could have done) — and spend your attention on setting up correctly and moving under control. You will get sore (that's DOMS, and it's normal and temporary), and you'll be tempted to add weight fast. Resist a little. A technique-first on-ramp makes everything after it work better.

**Quick recommendations**
- Keep loads **light and controlled — around 3–4 RIR** on every set for the first ~2 weeks. **[Grade D]**
- Treat each session as **skill practice**: nail the setup, the range of motion, and the tempo before you chase weight. **[Grade D]**
- **Show up on your scheduled days** even if a session feels easy — the habit is the point right now. **[Grade D]**
- Expect **muscle soreness (DOMS)** in the 1–2 days after training, especially early on; it fades as your body adapts. **[Grade C]**
- **Add weight gradually**, only once a movement feels smooth and repeatable — not on day two because it felt easy. **[Grade D]**

## Why hold back at the start

It feels backwards to go easy when you're motivated. But two things are true early on, and both argue for restraint.

First, **you're a beginner**, which is actually great news: beginners gain muscle and strength readily even with modest, sub-maximal loads, so you don't need to grind near failure to make progress right now. (More on why in [training status](../06-individualization/training-status.md).) Second, **your technique and your connective tissue haven't caught up to your enthusiasm yet.** Muscles adapt fast; tendons, joints, and skill take longer. Pushing heavy weight before the movement is grooved is how form breaks down and small tweaks happen. A gentle on-ramp lets everything catch up together.

## What to actually focus on

Spend the first two weeks getting these right, in order:

1. **Setup.** Where you sit, how you grip, where your feet go, how you brace. Getting into position correctly is half of good technique. If a machine has a seat or pad adjustment, learn your setting and remember it.
2. **Control.** Move the weight deliberately in both directions — lower it under control rather than letting it drop. You should be able to pause at any point.
3. **Range of motion.** Take each rep through a full, comfortable range rather than short, bouncy partials. Full-range reps are both better practice and better stimulus.
4. **Consistency.** Do this on every rep of every set. Grooving a clean, repeatable pattern now pays off for years.

Because you're staying at **3–4 RIR**, you'll have plenty of spare capacity to think about all of this. That's the point — light enough to learn, heavy enough to matter.

## Expect to be sore (it's normal)

A day or two after training, especially in these first weeks, muscles you worked will feel achy, tight, or tender when you move or press on them. This is **DOMS — delayed-onset muscle soreness** — and it is a normal response to unfamiliar effort, not a sign of injury. It typically shows up 12–48 hours later, peaks, and fades over a few days. It also **gets milder as your body adapts** — the same workout that wrecked you in week one barely registers by week three (the "repeated-bout effect").

A few honest notes: soreness is **not** a scorecard — you can build muscle without being sore, and being very sore doesn't mean the session was better. If soreness is severe enough to limit daily life, you did too much too soon; scale back next time. And sharp, sudden, or joint-centered pain is different from dull muscle soreness — that's a stop-and-assess signal, not DOMS. Full detail in [muscle soreness (DOMS)](../05-recovery/muscle-soreness-doms.md).

## Common mistakes

- **Adding weight because a session felt easy.** It's *supposed* to feel easy right now. Let the movement become second nature first.
- **Training to failure to "make it count."** At 3–4 RIR you're already making excellent beginner progress. Grinding just adds fatigue and risk.
- **Treating soreness as the goal.** Chasing maximum soreness leads to overreaching, not faster growth. Steady beats brutal.
- **Skipping sessions once the novelty fades.** The habit is the real adaptation you're building in weeks one and two. Show up.
- **Comparing yourself to experienced lifters.** Their loads reflect years of grooved technique and adapted tissue. Yours will get there the same way — one clean session at a time.
