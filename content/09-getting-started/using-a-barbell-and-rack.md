# Using a Barbell and Rack

> **TL;DR** — Loading a barbell safely is a short, learnable routine: put the **same weight on each side**, lock it in place with **collars** (the clips that stop plates sliding off), set the **J-hooks** (the arms that hold the resting bar) to about mid-chest height, and — most importantly — set the **safety pins/arms** (the catch bars in a power rack) to catch the barbell if you fail a rep. To know how much you are lifting, add the **empty bar (about 20 kg / 45 lb)** to the total of the plates. None of this is hard; doing it in the same order every time is what keeps you safe.

**Quick recommendations**
- Load **matching plates on both ends** and clip a **collar on each side** before every set. **[Grade D]**
- In a power rack, always **set the safety pins/arms just below your lowest good rep position** so the bar can never trap you. **[Grade D]**
- Set **J-hooks** so the resting bar sits at roughly **mid-chest to shoulder height** — high enough to reach, low enough to un-rack without tiptoeing. **[Grade D]**
- Compute your load as **bar weight + all plates**, and say the number to yourself before you lift. **[Grade D]**

## Loading the bar

1. **Balance both sides.** Whatever you put on the left, put the identical plate on the right. An unbalanced bar tips and is dangerous.
2. **Read the plate.** Plate weight is stamped or moulded into the metal (e.g. "20 kg", "45 lb", "10 kg"). Many gyms also colour-code them — in kilograms a common scheme is **red = 25 kg, blue = 20 kg, yellow = 15 kg, green = 10 kg** — but colours vary by brand, so **trust the printed number over the colour**.
3. **Slide plates on snugly** against the collar of the bar (the raised inner ring), heaviest first, then smaller plates outside them.

## Collars / clips — always use them

**Collars** (also called clips) are the fasteners that lock your plates onto the bar so they cannot slide off. Spring clips squeeze on; lock-jaw collars clamp shut. **Use them on every working set.** If you tip even slightly with no collar, plates slide off one end, that end shoots up, and the bar flips — a classic and avoidable accident. They take three seconds. Put them on.

## Computing the load

The number you are lifting is **the empty bar plus every plate on it.** A standard Olympic bar is about **20 kg (45 lb)**.

- Bar (20 kg) + one 20 kg plate each side = 20 + 20 + 20 = **60 kg**.
- Bar (45 lb) + a 45 lb plate each side = 45 + 45 + 45 = **135 lb** (the classic "one plate a side").

Say the total out loud before you get under it. It stops you loading the wrong weight and helps you track progress honestly — see [What to Track](../07-tracking/what-to-track.md).

## Setting the J-hooks

**J-hooks** are the J-shaped arms bolted to the rack that cradle the resting bar. Set their height so the bar sits around **mid-chest to shoulder height** for squats and presses: you want to lift the bar off the hooks with a small effort, not a big tiptoe reach, and re-rack it without hunting for the hooks. Set both hooks to the **same numbered hole** so the bar is level.

## Setting the safety pins/arms — the part that matters most

A **power rack** has a second set of horizontal catches — **safety pins, safety bars, or safety arms** — that sit below the bar and catch it if you cannot complete a rep. This is what lets you train hard alone.

- **Set them just below the lowest point of your good rep.** For a squat, that is a hair below the bar's position at the bottom of your deepest rep — low enough not to clip a normal rep, high enough that if you sink, the bar lands on the pins, not on you.
- **Test the height with an empty bar first.** Do one slow rep and check the bar clears the pins at the bottom but would be caught if you failed.
- Because the safeties mean a failed rep is harmless, they change how hard you can safely push — see [Proximity to Failure](../01-training-variables/proximity-to-failure.md).

If you have no rack, use lighter loads you can safely bail from, or work with a spotter.

## How the Smith machine's hooks work

On a **Smith machine** the bar runs on fixed rails and locks by a simple twist. Small hooks (pegs) stick out along the rails; you **rotate the bar a few degrees** to drop the hooks onto the nearest pegs and "park" it, and rotate it back to lift off. To bail, just twist and the bar catches on the next peg down — so the whole rail acts as a built-in safety. Rack it by twisting toward the hooks at the top of your last rep.

## Common mistakes

- **Skipping collars** "for just one set" — the one set is when a plate slides.
- **Uneven plates or mismatched J-hook holes**, leaving the bar tilted.
- **Setting safety pins too low** so they never actually catch you, or **too high** so they clip good reps.
- **Forgetting to add the 20 kg bar** when recording your weight, then wondering why your "numbers" jump around.
- **Un-racking a Smith bar without checking it is twisted off the hooks** — it will not move if it is still parked.
