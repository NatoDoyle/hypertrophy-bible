# Form Fundamentals

> **TL;DR** — Good form isn't complicated. **Control the weight** (no bouncing or heaving), use the **full range of motion you can control**, make the movement happen at the **joint the exercise is meant to train**, and **feel the target muscle working**. Do it the **same way every time** so your progress actually means something, and pick a load you can lift *well* — lighter with clean form beats heavier with sloppy form, every time. Learn these six ideas once and you can carry them to any exercise in the gym.

**Quick recommendations**
- **Control the weight** in both directions — lift it deliberately, lower it under control (about 1–2 seconds up, 2–3 seconds down). No bouncing, swinging, or dropping. **[Grade D]**
- Use the **fullest range of motion (ROM) you can control** — the full path the joint travels, from a good stretch to a full contraction. **[Grade C]**
- **Move at the intended joint.** A biceps curl bends the elbow; a squat bends the hips and knees. If other body parts start heaving, the weight's too heavy. **[Grade D]**
- **Keep tension on the target muscle** — think about the muscle you're training doing the work. **[Grade D]**
- **Be consistent** — same range, same tempo, same setup each session, so a heavier weight next week is real progress. **[Grade D]**
- **Leave your ego at the door.** Choose a load you can lift with all of the above intact. **[Grade D]**

## The six fundamentals

**Range of motion (ROM)** just means the distance a joint travels during a rep. Keep that phrase in mind — it comes up a lot below.

**1. Control the weight.** Every rep has two halves: the lift (the *concentric*, when the muscle shortens) and the lowering (the *eccentric*, when it lengthens). Move both deliberately. If you bounce out of the bottom or heave the weight up with momentum, momentum does the work instead of the muscle you're trying to grow. A calm tempo — roughly 1–2 seconds up, 2–3 seconds down — is a good default.

**2. Full range of motion you can control.** Take the working muscle from a good stretch to a full contraction on each rep. Deep, controlled reps generally build muscle better than short, bouncy "half reps." The caveat is *you can control* — only shorten the range if a position hurts a joint or you genuinely can't stay in control there.

**3. Move through the intended joint.** Each exercise is designed to move at a specific joint. A curl bends the elbow; a leg extension straightens the knee; a squat folds at the hips and knees. If you find your back swinging or your hips lurching to fling the weight, that's a sign the load is too heavy — drop it and let the right joint do the job.

**4. Keep tension on the target muscle.** Aim to feel the muscle you're training doing the work, rather than throwing the weight around with whatever helps. Avoid fully relaxing or resting at the easy point of the rep, which dumps the load onto your joints instead of the muscle.

**5. Consistent technique.** Do each exercise the same way — same depth, same tempo, same seat height and grip — session to session. This is what makes your training log trustworthy: if you go deep one week and shallow the next, a "heavier" number tells you nothing. Consistency is how you know progress is real.

**6. Leave your ego at the door.** This is the one that saves beginners the most trouble. A weight you can lift with clean form beats a heavier weight you have to cheat. Nobody is watching your numbers; they're watching whether you look in control. Start lighter than you think, earn the right to add weight, and you'll progress faster and get hurt less.

## How to check your own form

- **Film a set from the side** on your phone and watch it back — the camera is honest.
- **Slow down.** Most form problems disappear when you stop rushing.
- **If any principle breaks, drop the weight 10–20%** and rebuild the rep cleanly. **[Grade D]**
- **Ask.** A qualified coach or an experienced, trustworthy lifter can spot in seconds what a video can't.

## Common mistakes

- **Bouncing or using momentum** to move the weight instead of the muscle.
- **Half reps** — cutting the range short to lift more, then wondering why nothing grows.
- **Chasing the number** before the technique is solid.
- **Body english on isolation moves** — swinging the torso to finish a curl or lateral raise.
- **Inconsistent depth or setup**, which makes your log meaningless.
- **Rushing the lowering half** — the eccentric is training too; don't just drop the weight.
