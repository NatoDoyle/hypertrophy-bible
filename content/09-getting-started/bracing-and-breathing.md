# Bracing and Breathing

> **TL;DR** — To protect your spine when you lift, you *brace*: take a breath down into your belly and tighten your midsection as if you're about to be punched, then hold that tension through the hard part of the rep. On heavy sets, briefly holding that breath (the *Valsalva maneuver*) adds stability — but only for a second or two, and it isn't right for everyone (see the safety note). On lighter, higher-rep work you don't need to hold your breath at all; just keep your core firm and exhale as you push through the toughest point. Whenever another page tells you to "brace," this is what it means.

**Quick recommendations**
- Before a rep, breathe **into your belly** (not just your chest) and **tighten your midsection like you're bracing for a punch**. **[Grade D]**
- **Hold that brace through the hardest part** of the lift, then relax between reps or sets. **[Grade D]**
- On **heavy sets** (roughly 5 reps or fewer, or anything hard to stay stable under), briefly **hold the braced breath** during the rep — about 1–3 seconds — then breathe. **[Grade D]**
- On **lighter, higher-rep sets**, keep the brace but **breathe more freely — exhale as you push through the sticking point** (the hardest point in the range). **[Grade D]**
- If you have **high blood pressure, a heart condition, glaucoma, a history of fainting, or you're pregnant**, talk to a doctor before breath-holding under load. **[Grade D]**

## What "bracing" actually is

Your spine is a stack of bones that likes support when you put weight on it. You give it that support by turning your torso into a firm cylinder — this is called creating **intra-abdominal pressure** (pressure inside your belly that stiffens your trunk from the inside out). It sounds technical; it's really three steps.

1. **Breathe in low.** Take a breath down into your belly so your waist expands outward in every direction — front, sides, and back — not a shallow, high chest breath.
2. **Tighten everything.** Squeeze your abs and the muscles all the way around your waist as if someone's about to poke you in the stomach. You should feel firm 360 degrees around, not just in the front.
3. **Hold the tension** through the effort, keep your ribs down (don't flare them upward), then release once the hard part is done.

That's the whole cue. When a squat, deadlift, row, or overhead-press page says "brace," it means: belly breath, tighten, hold.

## The Valsalva maneuver (breath-holding on heavy sets)

On a genuinely heavy set, letting air out mid-rep makes your trunk go soft right when you need it stiffest. So many lifters **hold the braced breath** through the hardest part of the rep. This is the **Valsalva maneuver** — a brief breath-hold against a closed throat while you stay braced. It's the most stable way to move heavy loads.

How to do it: take your belly breath before the rep, brace, **hold for the 1–3 seconds** it takes to grind through the sticking point, then breathe out and reset for the next rep. Keep it short — a brief hold, not a marathon, and not across several reps in a row.

**Safety note — this is not medical advice.** Breath-holding under load briefly spikes your blood pressure. For healthy people lifting sensibly this is normal and self-limiting, but if you have **high blood pressure, any heart or cardiovascular condition, glaucoma, a history of fainting or dizziness, or you are pregnant**, check with a qualified doctor before using the Valsalva maneuver — and lean on the lighter-load breathing below instead. If you ever feel light-headed, rack the weight and sit down.

## Breathing on lighter, higher-rep work

For sets of roughly 10–20+ reps on machines, cables, or isolation moves, you don't need a hard breath-hold on every rep — it's tiring and unnecessary at those loads. Keep a **light brace** for control and **exhale through the sticking point** (breathe out as you push or pull through the hardest part), then inhale on the easier part. A simple default: **breathe out on the effort, breathe in on the way back.** Never hold your breath for a whole long set.

## Common mistakes

- **Chest-only breathing.** A shallow breath high in the chest doesn't stiffen your trunk. Breathe low, into the belly.
- **Only tightening the front.** Bracing is 360 degrees — sides and low back too. Don't suck your gut in; push out against your tightened muscles.
- **Flaring the ribs or over-arching.** Keep the ribcage stacked over the hips. A big backward arch isn't a brace.
- **Holding your breath for a whole set.** Fine for 1–3 seconds on a heavy rep; not fine for a 15-rep set — that's how you get dizzy.
- **Forgetting to brace on lighter lifts.** You still want a firm core on a row or a lunge, just with freer breathing.
- **Straining red-faced on every warm-up.** Save the full breath-hold for genuinely heavy work.
