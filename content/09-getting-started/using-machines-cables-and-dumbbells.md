# Using Machines, Cables, and Dumbbells

> **TL;DR** — Machines, cables, and dumbbells are the friendliest tools for a beginner, and each has one small setup skill. On a **machine**, read the little picture guide (the **placard**) and adjust the seat and pads so the machine's **pivot lines up with your joint**. On a **cable**, set the **pulley height**, clip on an **attachment**, choose your weight with the **stack pin**, and — this is the safety part — **control the weight back down** instead of letting it slam. With **dumbbells**, the trick is getting heavy ones into position with a **"kick-up"** and lowering them under control. Get these three setups right and most of the room opens up to you.

**Quick recommendations**
- On any machine, **match the moving pivot to the joint it bends** — line up the machine's hinge with your knee, elbow, or shoulder. **[Grade D]**
- Select load with the **stack pin**; use the small **micro-add-on** weights to make jumps smaller when a full plate is too big a step. **[Grade D]**
- **Lower every rep and the whole stack under control** — never let the weight stack crash down; that is where cables and machines hurt people. **[Grade D]**
- For dumbbell pressing, **kick the dumbbells up into position with your thighs**, and **sit down with them under control** to finish. **[Grade D]**

## Reading a machine placard

Most machines have a **placard** — a small printed panel showing a stick-figure using it correctly, which muscle it targets, and how to adjust it. Read it before you sit down. It tells you which lever adjusts the seat, where your body should touch the pads, and often which way to face. Thirty seconds here saves you from guessing.

## Adjusting the seat, pads, and pivot

The core idea for almost every machine: **the machine's pivot point (the bolt the arm rotates around) should sit at the same height as the joint you are bending.**

- **Leg extension** (works the thigh): the pivot should line up with your **knee**, and the ankle pad rest just above your foot.
- **Seated row / chest press** (back and chest): set the seat so the handles line up with the muscle you are working — roughly mid-chest for a press.
- **Preacher / arm machines**: the pivot lines up with your **elbow**.

Adjust the **seat height** first, then any **back or chest pad**, then leg or ankle pads. When the pivot and your joint match, the machine moves *with* your body and you feel the target muscle working through a full stretch and squeeze — which is what actually drives growth (see [Range of Motion](../01-training-variables/range-of-motion.md)). If it feels awkward or pinchy, the setup is usually off by a notch or two.

## Selecting the weight on a stack

A **weight stack** is a pile of labelled plates. You pick your load by pushing a **pin** (a metal peg) into the hole at the top of the weight you want — the pin picks up that plate and everything above it. Push it all the way in until it seats.

If jumping to the next plate is too big a leap (common for smaller muscles), most stacks have **micro add-ons** — small extra weights that hang on top or slot in, letting you add 1–2.5 kg instead of a full 5 kg plate. Use them; small jumps keep your progress smooth.

## Setting a cable pulley and choosing an attachment

A **cable machine** runs a cable from the stack over a **pulley** you can move up or down a vertical rail.

1. **Set the pulley height** for the movement: **low** for curls and rows, **high** for pulldowns and pressdowns, **mid** for things like cable flyes.
2. **Clip on the right attachment** — the handle you hold. A single **D-handle** for one arm, a **rope** for triceps and face pulls, a **straight or angled bar** for pushdowns. Attachments clip onto the cable's end clasp; make sure it is fully closed.
3. **Choose your weight with the stack pin** as above.
4. **Control the stack back down every rep.** When you finish a set, let the weight settle — do not release and let the whole stack slam. Slamming is loud, wears the machine, and is the main way people tweak a shoulder or finger on cables.

## Getting heavy dumbbells up — the "kick-up"

For dumbbell bench or shoulder pressing, the hardest part is getting heavy dumbbells into the start position without straining. The technique is the **kick-up**:

1. Sit on the end of the bench with a **dumbbell standing on each thigh**, close to your hips.
2. As you **lie back**, use your thighs to **kick / drive each dumbbell up** toward your shoulders, so you arrive lying down with the weights at chest or shoulder height, ready to press.
3. **To finish:** at the end of your last rep, bring the dumbbells back to your lower thighs, then **sit up as the weights carry you forward**, and set them down — or drop them safely to the sides if they are heavy and you are near failure. Do **not** try to slowly lower them overhead onto the floor; guide them down with control or let them go clear of your body.

Practise the kick-up with light dumbbells first so it is automatic before you go heavy.

## Common mistakes

- **Not adjusting the seat**, so the pivot sits above or below your joint and the movement feels wrong.
- **Only pushing the stack pin halfway in** — it can pop out mid-set.
- **Letting the stack slam** on the last rep of every cable set.
- **Clipping the wrong or unclosed attachment**, so a handle pops off under load.
- **Muscling heavy dumbbells up by hand** instead of using the kick-up, or **trying to lower them gently overhead** at the end of a hard set.
