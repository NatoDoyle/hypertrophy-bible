# Age

> **TL;DR** — You can build muscle at essentially **any age** — older adults gain muscle and strength from resistance training, and it's one of the most valuable things they can do for health and independence. Older lifters may need **more attention to recovery, joints, and per-meal protein**, and — notably — build muscle effectively even at **relatively low training volumes** (higher volumes mainly add *strength*). Adolescents can train safely with good coaching and technique. Age changes the details, not the fundamentals.

**Quick recommendations**
- **Older adults**: resistance-train — it builds muscle and protects function even at **low-to-moderate volumes**; add volume mainly when **strength** is the priority. Manage joints and recovery. **[Grade B]**
- Older lifters may need **more protein per meal** (~0.4+ g/kg) to overcome anabolic resistance (see [Protein](../04-nutrition/protein.md)). **[Grade B]**
- **Adolescents** can strength-train safely with supervision, technique focus, and sensible loads. **[Grade C]**
- Expect **slower recovery** with age — prioritize sleep and don't over-stack systemic fatigue. **[Grade C]**

## Practical Application

**Older adults (masters lifters).** Resistance training reverses much of age-related muscle loss (sarcopenia), improving strength, function, and quality of life. Key adjustments: emphasize **recovery** (recovery capacity declines with age), favor **joint-friendly exercise selection** (machines and controlled ranges as needed), keep **protein high with adequate per-meal doses**, and recognize that **even relatively low training volumes build muscle effectively** at this age (higher volumes chiefly add strength) — though the sets still need to be genuinely hard, not token effort. Progress may be a bit slower, but the trajectory is upward.

**Adolescents/youth.** Contrary to old myths, supervised resistance training is safe for young people and doesn't stunt growth. The emphasis should be on **technique, movement skill, and gradual progression** rather than maximal loads. Visible hypertrophy is more limited before/around puberty but strength and skill develop well.

## The Evidence

**Older adults build muscle effectively — and even relatively low volumes work well.** Radaelli and colleagues' 2025 network meta-analysis of resistance-training volume in older adults found that **low-volume resistance training was the most effective for lean body mass and lower-body hypertrophy**, whereas **higher volumes were needed mainly to maximize strength**[^radaelli-2025-older-adults-volume]. **[Grade B]** The practical message: older adults don't need high-volume bodybuilder programs to gain muscle — genuine, progressive resistance training at modest volumes drives hypertrophy, and you add volume chiefly when strength is the goal. Note that this **nuances the general volume dose-response** seen in younger and trained lifters (see [Volume](../01-training-variables/volume.md)) — most plausibly because recovery capacity declines with age — so this is a case where age genuinely shifts the prescription rather than just the details.

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance
- **The low-volume finding contrasts with the general dose-response** (where more volume helps younger/trained lifters). It likely reflects age-related recovery limits, and comes from a *network* meta-analysis (indirect comparisons), which carries more uncertainty than head-to-head trials — hence Grade B, not A.
- **Recovery slows with age**, so higher volumes in particular need more rest to recover from; individualize.
- **Anabolic resistance** means older muscle responds a bit less to a given protein dose — higher per-meal protein helps compensate.
- **Youth hypertrophy is hormone-limited** pre-puberty; strength/skill gains are the main early adaptations.
- **Health screening** is prudent for older or previously-sedentary beginners — this Bible is not medical advice.
