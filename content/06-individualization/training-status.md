# Training Status

> **TL;DR** — How trained you are changes what your program should look like. **Beginners** grow fast on modest volume and simple linear progression — they should not train like advanced bodybuilders. As you advance, growth slows and you need **more volume, more precise progression, and eventually specialization** to keep progressing. Match your program's complexity and volume to your training age, not to what an elite lifter does.

**Quick recommendations**
- **Beginners**: modest volume (~10 sets/muscle/week), simple progression, focus on technique and consistency. **[Grade B]**
- **Intermediates**: more volume, ~2× frequency, structured progression and periodic deloads. **[Grade B]**
- **Advanced**: higher volume, specialization phases, careful fatigue management — gains are slow and hard-won. **[Grade B]**
- Don't **copy elite routines** as a novice — they assume years of adaptation and recovery capacity. **[Grade C]**

## Practical Application

| Stage | Volume need | Progression | Priority |
|-------|-------------|-------------|----------|
| Beginner (0–1 yr) | Lower (~8–12 sets/muscle/wk) | Add load/reps most sessions | Technique, consistency, habit |
| Intermediate (1–3 yr) | Moderate–high | Double progression, deloads | Sufficient volume, ~2× frequency |
| Advanced (3+ yr) | High, individualized | Autoregulation, specialization | Bringing up weak points, fatigue management |

**Why it changes.** Beginners are far from their genetic ceiling, so almost any progressive stimulus works and recovery is quick — over-complicating things or piling on volume just adds fatigue without extra benefit. Advanced lifters are close to their ceiling; the easy gains are gone, so they need more (and smarter) volume, more precise progression, and prioritization of lagging muscles to squeeze out slow gains.

## The Evidence

**Trained lifters need more volume to keep growing.** Schoenfeld and colleagues (2019) found **higher volumes produced greater hypertrophy in resistance-trained men**[^schoenfeld-2019-volume-trained-men] — a contrast with beginners, who respond to relatively little. **[Grade B]** The general **dose-response** still applies at every stage, but where you sit on it (and how much you can recover from) shifts with training age[^schoenfeld-2017-volume-dose-response]. **[Grade A]** This is the evidence basis for scaling volume up as you advance rather than starting there.

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance
- **"Training age" is fuzzy** — calendar years matter less than how hard and consistently you've actually trained.
- **The beginner→advanced transition is gradual**, not a switch; adjust volume by your own rate of progress.
- **More volume for advanced lifters has a ceiling too** — it's "more than a beginner needs," not "unlimited."
- **Detrained former lifters behave like advanced-returning**, not beginners (see [Returning Lifters](returning-lifters-and-special-populations.md)).
