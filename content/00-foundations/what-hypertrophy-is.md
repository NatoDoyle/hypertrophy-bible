# What Hypertrophy Is

> **TL;DR** — Muscle hypertrophy is the **enlargement of existing muscle fibers**, driven mainly by adding **contractile proteins (myofibrils)** so fibers grow thicker. In humans, growing *new* fibers (hyperplasia) is negligible — you're making the fibers you have bigger, not making more of them. Because muscle size is measured indirectly (ultrasound, MRI, biopsy) and short-term measurements are confounded by swelling, **judge real growth over months, not weeks**.

**Quick recommendations**
- Think of your goal as increasing each muscle's **cross-sectional area** over time. **[Grade A]**
- Don't trust week-to-week size changes — **fluid, glycogen, and swelling** distort them. Track over 8–12+ weeks. **[Grade B]**
- Expect growth to be **slow and roughly linear-then-decelerating** across a training career (see [Stimulus, Fatigue & Adaptation](stimulus-fatigue-adaptation.md)). **[Grade B]**

## Practical Application

Hypertrophy is a structural change: muscle fibers accumulate more myofibrils (the contractile machinery) and expand in diameter, increasing the muscle's cross-sectional area and, eventually, its visible size. This is why **progressive overload** and **sufficient volume** matter — they are the signals that tell fibers to add protein.

What this means practically:

- **Size follows tension and volume, accumulated over time.** There is no single session that "builds" a visible amount of muscle; growth is the sum of many stimulating sessions.
- **Measurement is noisy.** A muscle can look bigger after training from a "pump" (transient fluid shift) or after carb-loading (glycogen pulls water into muscle) without any real fiber growth. Use consistent conditions (e.g. morning, unpumped) and long time windows.
- **Strength and size overlap but differ.** Early strength gains are largely neural (learning to produce force); measurable hypertrophy lags behind and continues as the driver of strength later.

## The Evidence

**Hypertrophy is primarily myofibrillar.** The dominant, best-supported form of growth is the accumulation of contractile proteins that thickens the fiber; comprehensive reviews of overload-induced hypertrophy treat myofibrillar expansion as the core adaptation[^roberts-2023-mechanisms-review]. **[Grade B]** A separate proposed component — **sarcoplasmic hypertrophy** (expansion of the fluid/non-contractile fraction) — is biologically plausible but its magnitude and trainability in humans remain debated. **[Grade C]**

**Measurement shapes the science.** Haun and colleagues (2019) argued that "hypertrophy" is a **construct measured differently by different tools** — biopsy (fiber cross-sectional area), ultrasound and MRI (whole-muscle size), and DXA (lean mass) — and that these do not always agree, partly because training-induced **muscle swelling and edema inflate short-term size measures**[^haun-2019-measurement]. **[Grade B]** This is why reputable studies use multi-week interventions and, ideally, more than one measurement method, and why you should judge your own progress over months.

**Hyperplasia is not a meaningful human mechanism.** Splitting of fibers to create new ones has been shown in some animal models under extreme conditions, but evidence for meaningful hyperplasia in trained humans is weak; the practical driver is enlargement of existing fibers[^roberts-2023-mechanisms-review]. **[Grade C]**

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance

- **Sarcoplasmic hypertrophy** — whether "non-functional" fluid growth is a real, trainable, distinct adaptation (and whether certain rep ranges bias it) is genuinely unresolved. Don't program around it.
- **Early size measurements overstate growth** because of edema from muscle damage — a key reason the muscle-damage-equals-growth idea is misleading (see [Mechanisms](mechanisms.md)).
- **Regional growth is non-uniform.** A muscle can grow more in some regions than others depending on exercise selection, so whole-muscle averages hide detail.
