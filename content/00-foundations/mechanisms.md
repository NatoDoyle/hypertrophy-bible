# Mechanisms: How Muscle Grows

> **TL;DR** — The primary signal for muscle growth is **mechanical tension** — hard contractions against meaningful resistance through a range of motion. Your muscles sense that tension and respond by ramping up **muscle protein synthesis (MPS)**; when synthesis outpaces breakdown over weeks, fibers get bigger. Two other factors — **metabolic stress** ("the burn") and **muscle damage** — are secondary and mostly ride along with hard training rather than being goals to chase. Notably, chasing soreness or a big "pump" for their own sake is not how you optimize growth.

**Quick recommendations**
- Prioritize **mechanical tension**: challenging loads, full range of motion, sets taken close to failure. **[Grade B]**
- Don't program *for* soreness or the pump — **muscle damage and metabolic stress are byproducts, not targets**. **[Grade B]**
- Trust the **long game**: growth is net protein balance summed over many sessions, not any single workout's MPS spike. **[Grade B]**

## Practical Application

Everything in [Training Variables](../01-training-variables/index.md) is downstream of one idea: **create high mechanical tension in the target muscle, repeatedly, and recover from it.** That's why volume (how many hard sets), proximity to failure (how much tension the fibers actually experience), and range of motion (tension at long muscle lengths) are the variables that move the needle — and why load itself is flexible, since near failure even lighter loads generate high tension in the working fibers.

The corollary is what *not* to optimize:

- **Soreness (DOMS) is not the target.** It reflects unaccustomed work and muscle damage, not the size of the growth stimulus.
- **The pump is not the target.** It's a transient metabolic/fluid effect; pleasant and possibly a minor contributor, but not something to chase at the expense of tension and volume.

## The Evidence

**Mechanical tension is the primary driver.** Reviews of the molecular basis of hypertrophy converge on mechanically-induced signaling as the initiating stimulus: muscles contain sensors that detect mechanical loading and trigger the growth response[^wackerhage-2019-stimuli-sensors]. Schoenfeld's framework similarly places mechanical tension first, with metabolic stress and muscle damage as secondary factors[^schoenfeld-2010-mechanisms], and the most comprehensive recent review reaffirms mechanical load as the central cause of overload hypertrophy[^roberts-2023-mechanisms-review]. **[Grade B]**

**Growth is net protein balance.** Resistance exercise elevates muscle protein synthesis for a period afterward; when the sum of these elevations exceeds protein breakdown across weeks, fibers accumulate protein and grow[^damas-2015-mps-review]. **[Grade A]** But the relationship between an *acute* MPS spike and *chronic* growth is not one-to-one.

**Early MPS is partly a repair signal, not a growth signal.** In an elegant time-course study, Damas and colleagues (2016) found that in the first weeks of training, much of the elevated myofibrillar protein synthesis was directed at **repairing muscle damage**, and MPS only correlated with actual hypertrophy *after* damage subsided[^damas-2016-mps-damage]. Their broader synthesis frames muscle damage as, at best, a permissive byproduct — not a driver — of growth[^damas-2018-hypertrophy-damage-mps]. **[Grade B]** This is a major reason "no pain, no gain" is wrong: the soreness of early or novel training reflects damage the body must fix before it can build.

**Metabolic stress: a modest, secondary contributor.** The accumulation of metabolites during sustained contractions (the mechanism behind "the burn" and the pump) may contribute to hypertrophy through several proposed pathways — increased fiber recruitment, cell swelling, and signaling — but the evidence positions it as a secondary modulator, not a primary cause[^schoenfeld-2013-metabolic-stress]. **[Grade C]**

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance

- **The exact molecular chain from tension to growth is still being mapped.** We know tension is primary and involves mechanosensing and mTOR-related signaling, but the full pathway and the relative weight of each input are active research areas[^roberts-2023-mechanisms-review].
- **Metabolic stress vs tension is hard to isolate** in practice, because hard sets create both. This is fine — training hard delivers the primary stimulus regardless.
- **Muscle damage is not required and may be counterproductive in excess**, because recovering from damage competes with adaptation and limits training frequency and volume (see [Recovery → DOMS](../05-recovery/index.md)).
- **Acute MPS is a poor proxy for long-term growth** — don't over-interpret single-session synthesis studies.
