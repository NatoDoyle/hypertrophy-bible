# Reading the Evidence

> **TL;DR** — This Bible grades every claim A–D and cites real studies, but you should know **how to read that evidence** — because the hypertrophy literature has real limitations. Most studies are **short (8–12 weeks), small, done in untrained men, and measure size with noisy tools**. Effect sizes for many "hot" variables are **small**. So the honest hierarchy is: trust the **big, replicated findings** (volume matters, load is flexible, protein ~1.6 g/kg), hold the **fashionable details** loosely (lengthened partials, exact RIR), and always weight **your own results** over any single study. Certainty is a spectrum, and the evidence grades on each page tell you where a claim sits.

**Quick recommendations**
- Weight claims by their **evidence grade** (A→D) and by **replication across independent groups**, not by how exciting they sound. **[Grade C]**
- Be skeptical of **single studies and fashionable findings** — wait for replication before overhauling your training. **[Grade C]**
- Treat **effect sizes as usually small** — most training-variable tweaks change outcomes modestly, not dramatically. **[Grade C]**
- Trust **your own tracked results** over any study when they conflict (see [Tracking](../07-tracking/index.md)). **[Grade C]**

## Practical Application

**How to read a claim in this Bible (or anywhere):**

| Signal | What it tells you |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Grade A** (multiple RCTs/meta-analyses, replicated) | Act on it confidently — it's about as settled as the field gets |
| **Grade B** (several studies, some inconsistency) | A reasonable default that may shift with new evidence |
| **Grade C** (few/mixed studies, mostly untrained/short) | Provisional — hold it loosely |
| **Grade D** (mechanism/practice, little direct RCT support) | Reasoned, not proven — a working assumption |
| **Independent replication** (different research groups agree) | Much stronger than one group repeating itself |
| **Small effect size** | Real but minor — don't reorganize your program around it |

**The practical filter:** the *fundamentals* (do enough hard volume, eat enough protein, progress over time, sleep) are Grade A and won't change. The *details* people argue about online (exact RIR, lengthened partials, tempo, timing) are mostly Grade B–D with small effects — worth applying, not worth agonizing over. When a flashy new study contradicts a large body of evidence, the large body usually wins until the finding replicates.

## The Evidence

**The literature's limitations are real and worth naming.** Most resistance-training studies are **short** (commonly 6–12 weeks), **small** (often a few dozen participants), and disproportionately conducted in **young, untrained men** — so they may not translate cleanly to trained lifters, women, or older adults, and they can't capture multi-year adaptation. On top of that, **measuring hypertrophy is noisy**: Haun and colleagues showed that size estimates differ by method (biopsy, ultrasound, MRI, DXA) and are confounded by short-term swelling and fluid shifts[^haun-2019-measurement]. **[Grade B]** These constraints are why so many findings land at Grade B/C rather than A, why **meta-analyses** (which pool many studies) are weighted heavily here, and why **independent replication across research groups** is treated as a strong signal (and why this Bible deliberately cites multiple groups, not one). Where the evidence is genuinely thin or mixed — female-specific hypertrophy, exact volume ceilings, the magnitude of lengthened-position benefits — the pages say so rather than manufacturing false certainty. **[Grade C]**

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance
- **"No strong evidence" ≠ "doesn't work."** Absence of high-quality studies (common for niche topics) means uncertainty, not disproof — hence Grade D for reasoned-but-untested practices.
- **Generalizability is a recurring gap** — results from untrained young men may over- or under-state effects in trained lifters, women, and older adults.
- **Publication and novelty bias** push exciting findings into the spotlight before they replicate; be patient.
- **Your n = 1 matters** — population averages guide the starting point, but your own tracked response is the final authority for *your* training.
