# Exercise Selection & Order

> **TL;DR** — Pick exercises that (1) **load the target muscle through its functions, especially in the lengthened position**, (2) are **stable enough to push close to failure** safely, and (3) together cover a muscle's **regions and a range of joint angles**. Free weights and machines both build muscle — choose per exercise, not by tribe. **Exercise order** has only modest effects: do your **highest-priority and most fatiguing (usually compound) work first**, while you're fresh, and accept that whatever comes last gets slightly less. Consistency on a core set of exercises beats constant novelty.

**Quick recommendations**
- Choose exercises that **load the muscle when it's long** and match its strength curve (see [Range of Motion](range-of-motion.md)). **[Grade B]**
- Favor exercises **stable enough to train near failure** without balance or technique being the limit. **[Grade C]**
- Cover a muscle's **functions and regions** with 2–4 exercises rather than hammering one angle. **[Grade C]**
- **Order by priority and fatigue**: hardest/most important lifts first; isolation and lower-priority work later. **[Grade D]**
- Keep a **stable core of exercises** and progress them; rotate only occasionally for variety, joint health, or a stalled lift. **[Grade C]**

## Practical Application

**A four-filter checklist for picking an exercise:**

1. **Does it load the target muscle well — especially lengthened?** Prefer resistance profiles that challenge the muscle in its stretched position (overhead triceps, RDLs for hamstrings, deep presses/flyes for chest).
2. **Can you push it hard and safely?** An exercise you can take to ~1–2 RIR with confidence delivers more stimulus than one limited by balance, grip, or lower-back fatigue. This is where machines and cables shine.
3. **What does it add to the muscle's coverage?** Combine a lengthened-biased compound with complementary angles/regions (e.g. incline + flat + a fly for chest; a vertical + horizontal pull for back).
4. **Can you progress it over time?** You need to add load or reps on it week to week — so it should be repeatable and measurable.

**Free weights vs machines.** Neither is superior in general. Free weights offer loaded stretch, long ranges, and skill transfer; machines offer stability, constant tension, and safe failure. A good program uses both: compounds (often free-weight) to build the bulk of the work, machines/cables to safely target muscles and train the stretch. See the free-weights-vs-machines entry in [Myths](../08-myths/index.md).

**Ordering a session.** Put the exercises that demand the most fresh performance — heavy compounds, and anything for a **priority/lagging muscle** — early. Do smaller isolation and lower-priority work later. If a muscle is your main goal, you can (and sometimes should) break "compounds always first" and train it first while fresh.

## The Evidence

**Selection changes *where* and *how much* a muscle is stimulated.** The lengthened-position research applies directly here: Maeo and colleagues (2023) showed that simply changing arm position — overhead versus neutral — **substantially changed triceps growth** for the same movement[^maeo-2023-triceps-overhead], and Pedrosa and colleagues (2022) showed loading at long muscle lengths drives favorable adaptations[^pedrosa-2022-lengthened-partials]. Kassiano and colleagues' 2023 review reinforces that **the position of loading within the range is a real selection variable**, not a detail[^kassiano-2023-rom-review]. **[Grade B]** Mechanistically, this fits tension being the primary hypertrophy stimulus[^schoenfeld-2010-mechanisms] — exercises that maximize tension on the target muscle through its range are the better picks. **[Grade C]**

**Exercise order: modest and mostly about freshness.** There isn't strong hypertrophy-specific evidence that a particular order is optimal; the reliable finding across the training literature is simpler — **whatever you do earlier in a session is performed with more capacity, and whatever comes last is done with accumulated fatigue.** The practical implication (train priorities and the most fatiguing lifts first) follows from that, so it's graded as **practice-based [Grade D]** rather than a strongly evidenced law. Don't over-invest in ordering; invest in effort and volume.

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance

- **"Best exercise" is individual.** Limb lengths, joint tolerances, and where you *feel* a muscle working all shift the ideal choice. Two well-selected exercises for the same muscle can be roughly interchangeable.
- **Variety is a seasoning, not a strategy.** Some rotation across a training career aids complete development and joint health, but constantly swapping exercises undermines progressive overload — you can't progress what you don't repeat (see the "muscle confusion" myth).
- **Order effects are small.** Beyond "priorities first," the exact sequence rarely makes or breaks results; fatigue management across the week matters more than order within a session.
- **Machines aren't "lesser."** Their stability is an advantage for training a muscle hard and safely, not a compromise.
