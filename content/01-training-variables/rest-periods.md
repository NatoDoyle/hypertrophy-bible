# Rest Periods

> **TL;DR** — Rest **long enough to perform your next set well** — usually **~2–3 minutes on compounds** and **~1–2 minutes on isolation**. The old idea that short rests grow more muscle (via a bigger "pump" or hormone surge) is backward: **short rests cut the reps and load you can produce on later sets, reducing effective volume**. Longer rests preserve performance and support at least as much (often more) hypertrophy. Rest by *readiness*, not a stopwatch.

**Quick recommendations**
- Rest **~2–3 min between hard compound sets**; **~1–2 min** is fine for isolation. **[Grade B]**
- Rest until you can **hit your target reps at the target effort** on the next set — that's the real rule. **[Grade B]**
- Don't deliberately cut rest short to chase a pump or "fat-burning"; it mostly just **lowers your volume**. **[Grade B]**
- Use **shorter rests only to save time** on lower-stakes accessory work, accepting slightly fewer reps. **[Grade C]**

## Practical Application

Rest length is really a **performance-recovery** decision. Between hard sets, three things need to recover enough for a quality next set: your working muscles, your breathing/cardiovascular system, and (on big lifts) your central drive and technique. Bigger, more systemic exercises need more of all three.

| Exercise | Typical rest |
|----------|--------------|
| Heavy multi-joint (squat, deadlift, press, row) | 2–3 min (sometimes more) |
| Moderate compounds and machine presses/rows | 1.5–2.5 min |
| Isolation (curls, extensions, raises, calves) | 1–2 min |
| Antagonist or non-competing supersets | Alternate to save time without sacrificing per-set quality |

**Superset smartly.** Pairing exercises that don't compete (e.g. a calf raise between back sets, or biceps and triceps) lets you save time without shortening the *effective* rest for either muscle. Supersetting two exercises for the *same* muscle, by contrast, sacrifices load and reps.

## The Evidence

**Longer rest supported more growth, not less.** Schoenfeld and colleagues (2016) randomized resistance-trained men to **1-minute versus 3-minute** rest intervals with an otherwise identical program and found the **3-minute group gained more muscle and strength**[^schoenfeld-2016-rest-intervals]. **[Grade B]** The mechanism is straightforward: with only a minute of rest, later sets suffered large drops in reps, so the short-rest group accumulated less total high-quality work — and effective [volume](volume.md) is what drives growth. The extra metabolic stress from short rest did not compensate.

This overturned the long-standing bodybuilding belief that short rests are superior for hypertrophy (via greater metabolic stress and acute growth-hormone release). Since the acute hormonal response is not a meaningful driver of hypertrophy (see [Intensity](intensity-load-and-rep-ranges.md) and [Myths](../08-myths/index.md)), and short rests demonstrably reduce per-set performance, the case for deliberately short rests to build muscle is weak.

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance

- **The evidence base is smaller than for volume or load.** The direction (longer ≥ shorter for hypertrophy) is well-supported and mechanistically sensible, but the precise optimal duration is not finely resolved — hence "rest by readiness."
- **Well-trained isolation work tolerates shorter rest.** For small muscles on machines, 60–90 seconds often restores enough performance; the cost of short rest is smaller when the exercise is less systemically fatiguing.
- **Time efficiency is legitimate.** If time is limited, slightly shorter rests on accessories (accepting a few fewer reps) is a reasonable trade — just don't do it on your heaviest, highest-value sets.
- **"Enough rest" rises with load and set number.** Later sets and heavier loads generally need more rest to maintain output.
