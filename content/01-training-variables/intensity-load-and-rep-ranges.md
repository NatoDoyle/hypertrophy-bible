# Intensity / Load & Rep Ranges

> **TL;DR** — For building muscle, the **load you use is remarkably flexible**. Anywhere from roughly **5 to 30 reps per set builds similar hypertrophy — as long as sets are taken close to failure**. What matters is hard effort against meaningful tension, not a magic rep range. Practically, most work sits in **~6–12 reps** because it's time-efficient and technique-friendly (with isolation and machine work often going a bit higher, ~12–20); save very heavy low reps for strength and compounds, and very light high reps for small muscles and finishing work. If you want maximal *strength*, that specifically favors heavier loads.

**Quick recommendations**
- Train across **~5–30 reps** and expect similar muscle growth when sets are hard. **[Grade A]**
- Anchor most compound and moderate work in **~6–12 reps** (isolation and machines often **12–20**) for the best balance of stimulus, fatigue, joint stress, and time. **[Grade B]**
- Go **heavier (~3–6 reps)** on some big compounds if you also care about strength; go **lighter (~15–30)** on isolation and machines where heavy loading is awkward or joint-stressful. **[Grade B]**
- Keep load **above ~30% of 1RM**; extremely light loads (~20% 1RM) grow muscle worse even to failure. **[Grade B]**
- Whatever the load, the **last few reps near failure are what count** — see [Proximity to Failure](proximity-to-failure.md). **[Grade B]**

## Practical Application

**Pick load by exercise, not by dogma.** Choose a rep range that lets you train a given exercise hard with good technique and tolerable joint stress:

| Rep range | Best for | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| 3–6 | Heavy compounds (squat, press, row, deadlift) | Builds strength; more CNS/joint fatigue per hard set; more warm-up needed |
| 6–12 | The default for most exercises | Efficient stimulus per unit fatigue; easy to progress |
| 12–20 | Isolation, machines, cables | Great where heavy loading is awkward or joint-stressful (e.g. lateral raises, leg extensions) |
| 20–30 | Small muscles, finishing work | Works if taken near failure; the last reps get very uncomfortable |

**Why the range is so wide.** Muscle growth is driven primarily by **mechanical tension** through a working range, and near failure the muscle recruits and fatigues its high-threshold motor units regardless of whether the bar is heavy-and-slow or lighter-and-faster. So the *load* is a dial you set for practicality; the *effort* is what you must not compromise.

**Strength is the exception.** If a specific one-rep-max matters to you, heavier training is meaningfully better — strength is partly a skill of expressing force against heavy loads. You can pursue both by using heavier ranges on key lifts and moderate-to-light ranges elsewhere.

## The Evidence

**Load barely affects hypertrophy when effort is matched.** Schoenfeld, Grgic, Ogborn, and Krieger's 2017 meta-analysis found that low-load (<60% 1RM) and high-load (>60% 1RM) training produce **similar hypertrophy** when sets are taken to failure, while **strength gains favored heavy loads**[^schoenfeld-2017-load-meta]. **[Grade A]** A later volume-matched meta-analysis by Carvalho and colleagues (2022) reached the same split conclusion: comparable growth across loads, greater maximal-strength gains with heavier loads[^carvalho-2022-volume-matched-loads]. **[Grade A]**

**Even the hormone story doesn't rescue "you must lift heavy."** Morton and colleagues (2016) trained resistance-trained men with either higher-load/lower-rep or lower-load/higher-rep protocols to failure and found **neither load nor the post-exercise rise in testosterone/growth hormone determined hypertrophy** — both groups grew similarly[^morton-2016-load-hormones]. **[Grade B]** This is a cornerstone against the acute-hormone myth (see [Myths](../08-myths/index.md)).

**There is a lower bound, though.** Lasevicius and colleagues (2018), with volume-load equated, found that **20% of 1RM produced less hypertrophy than 40%, 60%, or 80%**, which grew muscle similarly — evidence for a practical floor somewhere around 30–40% 1RM below which the stimulus weakens even at high effort[^lasevicius-2018-intensities-volume-load]. **[Grade B]**

**Moderate reps are usually more efficient.** Schoenfeld and colleagues (2014) compared a bodybuilding-style (3×10) versus powerlifting-style (7×3) program with volume equated: **hypertrophy was similar, but the heavy low-rep program took far longer per session and produced more joint discomfort and attrition**[^schoenfeld-2014-loading-strategies]. **[Grade B]** Similar growth, more cost — hence the moderate-rep default.

**The mechanistic backdrop.** Schoenfeld's review of hypertrophy mechanisms frames mechanical tension as the primary driver, with metabolic stress and muscle damage as secondary/modulating factors[^schoenfeld-2010-mechanisms] — consistent with why effort across a broad load range works, and why chasing "the pump" or soreness for their own sake is misguided. **[Grade C]**

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance

- **"To failure" is doing heavy lifting in these studies.** The load-agnostic result depends on comparable effort. Lighter loads only match heavier ones when taken close to failure — which is more uncomfortable and rep-heavy at light loads (see [Proximity to Failure](proximity-to-failure.md)).
- **Very high reps are hard to push.** Sets of 25–30 become limited by breathing and discomfort before the target muscle is fully taxed, which can blunt the stimulus in practice even if not in theory.
- **Fiber-type "rep range" claims are weak.** The idea that low reps preferentially grow Type II and high reps grow Type I fibers has some mechanistic plausibility but limited and inconsistent human evidence. Don't build a program around it. **[Grade C]**
- **Individual joint tolerance matters.** The "best" range for an exercise is partly the one your joints tolerate for hard, repeatable sets.
