# Tempo / Rep Duration

> **TL;DR** — Don't overthink tempo. Rep durations across a broad range — roughly **0.5 to ~8 seconds per rep** — build **similar amounts of muscle**. What you should do is **control the weight (especially the lowering phase) and avoid using momentum**, then focus your attention on hard, near-failure sets. **Deliberately super-slow reps (10+ seconds) are counterproductive** — they force you to use much lighter loads and do fewer quality reps without any growth advantage. "Time under tension" is not a target to maximize.

**Quick recommendations**
- Use a **controlled eccentric (~1–3 s)** and a **deliberate concentric** — roughly a "1–3 seconds down, lift with control" tempo. **[Grade C]**
- **Avoid super-slow training** (e.g. 10 s per phase); it lowers load and reps for no hypertrophy benefit. **[Grade B]**
- **Don't chase time-under-tension** as a goal — total hard, near-failure work matters, not seconds per rep. **[Grade B]**
- **Never bounce or heave** the weight; momentum offloads the muscle you're trying to train. **[Grade D]**

## Practical Application

Think "**controlled, not slow**." Lower the weight under control so the target muscle does the work — typically taking about 1–3 seconds — then lift it with intent. You don't need to count tempo like a metronome; you need to (1) not let gravity drop the weight, and (2) not throw it up with momentum. Within those bounds, natural rep speed is fine.

| Tempo approach | Verdict |
|----------------|---------|
| Explosive-but-controlled concentric, controlled eccentric | Good default |
| Moderate, controlled reps (~2 s down, ~1–2 s up) | Good default |
| Deliberate super-slow (5–10+ s per phase) | Avoid — big load penalty, no growth benefit |
| Bouncing, jerking, dropping the weight | Avoid — reduces effective tension |

**Where a controlled eccentric earns its keep** is technique and tension, not a stopwatch metric: it keeps the muscle loaded through the [range of motion](range-of-motion.md), reduces cheating, and makes each rep count toward the near-failure reps that drive growth.

## The Evidence

**A wide tempo range works equally well.** Schoenfeld, Ogborn, and Krieger's 2015 systematic review and meta-analysis on repetition duration concluded that **hypertrophy is similar across rep durations from roughly 0.5 to 8 seconds**, and that **very slow durations (>10 seconds per rep) are inferior**[^schoenfeld-2015-rep-duration-meta]. **[Grade B]** In other words, once you're controlling the weight, the exact speed is not a meaningful growth variable — but deliberately grinding every rep to maximize "time under tension" backfires because it forces much lighter loads and fewer effective reps.

This is why this Bible treats tempo as a **technique guideline** (control the eccentric, don't use momentum) rather than a programmed variable to manipulate for extra growth.

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance

- **Eccentric emphasis is plausibly minor, not magic.** Lengthening (eccentric) actions contribute to growth, but a normally controlled eccentric already captures this — exaggerated slow eccentrics don't add a proven bonus for the load penalty they impose.
- **Explosive intent ≠ sloppy reps.** Trying to move the concentric quickly (while the weight itself may not move fast under heavy load) is fine and may aid strength; it's the *loss of control* that hurts, not speed itself.
- **Tempo can be a tool for other goals.** Slowing down can help groove technique, manage a cranky joint, or increase difficulty when equipment is limited — just not as a hypertrophy optimizer.
