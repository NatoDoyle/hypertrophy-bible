# Detraining & Maintenance

> **TL;DR** — Muscle is **much easier to maintain than to build**. You can hold onto your gains with **far less training than it took to build them** — often as little as **~1/3 the volume, or one session a week**, *as long as you keep the intensity (load) up*. If you stop completely, muscle loss is **gradual** (noticeable after a few weeks, not days), and whatever you lose comes back **quickly** when you return (muscle memory). So during busy periods, **don't quit — downshift to a maintenance dose.**

**Quick recommendations**
- To **maintain** muscle when time is short, cut volume/frequency hard but **keep training with meaningful load**. **[Grade B]**
- A **minimal maintenance dose** (roughly a third of your usual volume, even ~1×/week per muscle) holds muscle for weeks–months. **[Grade C]** *(the direct evidence is for strength/performance; the exact "~1/3 volume" figure for muscle size is an informed extrapolation — see The Evidence)*
- Don't panic over a **short break** — meaningful muscle loss takes weeks, and regain is fast (see [Muscle Memory](../00-foundations/satellite-cells-and-muscle-memory.md)). **[Grade C]**
- Use the **maintenance-volume (MV)** landmark in the [muscle data](../../data/muscles/) as your "hold" target. **[Grade C]**

## Practical Application

**Maintenance ≠ growth volume.** Building muscle takes progressive, sufficient volume; *keeping* it takes surprisingly little. The key is that **intensity (load) is what preserves adaptations** — you can slash the number of sets and sessions, but the sets you do keep should still be reasonably heavy/hard. Practical maintenance modes:

- **Busy week / travel:** drop to ~1 hard session per muscle, a few heavy sets each — enough to hold, not to grow.
- **Deload or minor injury elsewhere:** maintain the unaffected muscles with reduced volume while you manage the issue.
- **Long layoff (illness, life):** something beats nothing — even brief, infrequent heavy exposure slows loss.

**If you do detrain fully:** loss is gradual — strength is retained longer than size, and noticeable atrophy generally takes a few weeks of complete cessation, not days. And because previously-built muscle regrows fast, a comeback is an **accelerated ramp**, not a restart (see [Returning Lifters](../06-individualization/returning-lifters-and-special-populations.md)).

## The Evidence

**A small dose maintains what a large dose built.** Spiering and colleagues' 2021 review on the **minimal dose to preserve strength and performance** concluded that **substantially reduced training frequency and volume can maintain strength for extended periods — provided training intensity is preserved**[^spiering-2021-minimal-dose]. **[Grade B]** Note that review is directly about *strength/performance* maintenance; extending it to **muscle size** is a reasonable, mechanistically-supported extrapolation rather than a finding from a direct reduced-volume *hypertrophy* trial — so read the exact "~1/3 volume" figure as an informed estimate, not a measured hypertrophy threshold. The practical logic holds either way: you built the muscle with your full [volume](../01-training-variables/volume.md) prescription, but you can *hold* it with far less as long as the remaining work stays hard. Combined with the muscle-memory biology — retained myonuclei make regrowth fast after a break[^bruusgaard-2010-myonuclei] — the practical message is reassuring: interruptions cost less than people fear, and downshifting beats stopping. **[Grade C]**

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance
- **"Maintenance" has a time limit** — a minimal dose holds muscle for weeks to a few months; indefinite minimal training may slowly erode gains.
- **Intensity is the non-negotiable** — you can cut sets and sessions, but dropping the load too is when maintenance fails.
- **Rate-of-loss specifics vary** by individual, age, and how the break is spent (total rest vs reduced training, nutrition, activity).
- **Strength outlasts size** — you may look smaller before you feel weaker during a layoff, partly due to fluid/glycogen changes.
