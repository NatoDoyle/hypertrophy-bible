# Muscle Soreness (DOMS)

> **TL;DR** — Delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS) is **not a measure of a good workout**, and chasing it is a mistake. Soreness mainly reflects **unaccustomed or novel work and the muscle damage it causes** — it fades as you adapt, even as you keep growing. Early in training (or after a new exercise), much of your body's repair effort goes to fixing damage rather than building new muscle. You can grow with little soreness, and being very sore doesn't mean you grew more.

**Quick recommendations**
- **Don't use soreness to judge a session** or program — it tracks novelty and damage, not growth. **[Grade B]**
- Expect soreness to **decrease as you adapt** to an exercise (the "repeated-bout effect") — that's normal, not a plateau. **[Grade C]**
- Avoid **excessive muscle damage** (huge novel eccentric overload) that impairs the next sessions. **[Grade C]**
- Manage soreness with **light movement and time**; it's not something you need to eliminate. **[Grade C]**

## Practical Application

Soreness is a poor training gauge. A brand-new exercise will make you sore regardless of whether it's an effective growth stimulus; a familiar exercise you're progressing well on may barely make you sore while driving real growth. Use **performance (load and reps going up over time)** as your gauge, not next-day pain.

Practical implications:
- **Don't program *for* soreness.** Constantly switching exercises to "feel it" the next day sabotages progression (see [Variation & Specialization](../03-programming/variation-and-specialization.md)) and just keeps you in the high-damage phase.
- **Very high muscle damage is counterproductive**, because recovering from it competes with adaptation and can compromise your next session's quality and volume.
- **Soreness will diminish** as you repeat an exercise — this is expected adaptation, not a sign you need novelty.

## The Evidence

**Early training MPS goes to repair, not growth.** Damas and colleagues (2016) tracked untrained lifters and found that in the first weeks, elevated muscle protein synthesis was substantially directed at **repairing muscle damage**, and only correlated with actual hypertrophy **after the damage (and soreness) subsided**[^damas-2016-mps-damage]. **[Grade B]** Their broader synthesis concludes that **muscle damage is not a driver of hypertrophy** — at best a byproduct, and in excess a hindrance[^damas-2018-hypertrophy-damage-mps]. **[Grade B]** This directly refutes "soreness = growth": the soreness of novel training marks the damage phase, during which growth is *lower*, not higher.

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance
- **Some damage is unavoidable and not harmful** — the goal is not zero soreness, just not *chasing* it.
- **The repeated-bout effect is robust** — the same workout produces far less soreness the second time; this is adaptation, and it doesn't mean the workout stopped working.
- **Individual soreness sensitivity varies** — some people get sore easily and shouldn't read much into it.
- **Lengthened-position and heavy-eccentric work** can be more damaging; introduce them gradually (see [Range of Motion](../01-training-variables/range-of-motion.md)).
