# Timing & Hormone Myths

> **TL;DR** — The **"anabolic window"** — the idea that you must consume protein within minutes of training or lose gains — is a myth; the window is hours wide and total daily intake dominates. The belief that **training-induced spikes in testosterone and growth hormone build muscle** is also wrong: those acute hormonal blips don't meaningfully drive hypertrophy. And **fasted training or "no carbs at night"** won't sabotage your gains — meal timing is a minor factor next to your daily totals.

**Quick recommendations**
- Ignore the **anabolic window** — just hit your **daily protein** with meals around training. **[Grade A]**
- Don't chase **hormone spikes** (short rest, specific exercises) for growth — they don't drive it. **[Grade B]**
- **Fasted training and late-night carbs are fine** — timing is a minor lever. **[Grade B]**
- Spend your effort on **training, total protein, and calories** — the things that actually matter. **[Grade A]**

## Practical Application

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| Anabolic window — slam protein post-workout | The window is hours wide; daily protein and calories dominate |
| Training testosterone/GH spikes build muscle | Acute hormonal spikes don't meaningfully drive hypertrophy |
| Fasted training kills gains | Fine if daily protein/calories are met |
| No carbs at night / carbs after 6pm store as fat | Timing of carbs doesn't determine fat gain; total energy does |

The unifying truth: **daily totals beat timing.** Get enough protein (~1.6 g/kg) and set your calories for your goal, and the clock is a rounding error.

## The Evidence

**The anabolic window is wide.** Schoenfeld, Aragon, and Krieger's meta-analysis found **protein timing had no significant independent effect on hypertrophy once total daily protein was controlled** — apparent timing benefits were explained by the timing groups eating more protein[^schoenfeld-2013-protein-timing-meta]. **[Grade A]** This also dispatches the "must eat immediately / can't train fasted / no late carbs" worries: what matters is the daily total, not the minute.

**Hormone spikes don't build muscle.** Morton and colleagues (2016) trained men and found that **neither the training load nor the post-exercise rise in testosterone and growth hormone determined hypertrophy or strength gains**[^morton-2016-load-hormones]. **[Grade B]** The transient hormonal surges from certain protocols (short rest, big-muscle exercises) are real but do not meaningfully cause growth — so programming to maximize them (e.g. deliberately short rests) is misguided (see [Rest Periods](../01-training-variables/rest-periods.md)).

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance
- **Peri-workout nutrition matters at the extremes** — twice-daily or very long training, or fully fasted sessions — but not for typical trainees eating regular meals.
- **Chronically elevated hormones (e.g. anabolic drugs) absolutely affect muscle** — the myth is specifically about the small, acute, training-induced spikes.
- **A pre-sleep protein feeding** can help *total* intake, which is the real (modest) benefit — not magic overnight timing.
