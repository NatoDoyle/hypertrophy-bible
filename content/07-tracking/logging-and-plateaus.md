# Logging & Plateaus

> **TL;DR** — Keep a **training log** — it's what makes progressive overload possible, because you can't beat a number you didn't record. Use it to progress each session and to diagnose **plateaus**. A real plateau (no progress over several weeks despite good effort) usually means one of a few fixable things: not enough **volume**, not enough **effort/overload**, too much **fatigue**, or stalled **exercise/technique** — address them in that order before assuming you've hit your ceiling.

**Quick recommendations**
- **Log every working set** (exercise, load, reps, RIR) so you can progress against last time. **[Grade A]**
- Define a plateau as **no progress over ~3–4 weeks** at good effort — not one bad session. **[Grade C]**
- Troubleshoot in order: **add volume → increase effort/overload → manage fatigue (deload) → change exercise**. **[Grade C]**
- Distinguish a **true plateau** from **noise** (a bad week, poor sleep, under-eating) before overhauling. **[Grade C]**

## Practical Application

**The log is the tool.** Progressive overload is only possible if you know what you did last time. Record exercise, load, reps, and RIR each session; then your job each week is simply to beat the log — one more rep, a little more load, or one more quality set.

**Diagnosing a plateau.** If a lift (or a muscle's growth) genuinely stalls for several weeks despite consistent, hard training, work through likely causes in order of impact:

| Check | Fix |
|-------|-----|
| Enough volume? | Add 1–3 sets/week to the muscle ([Volume](../01-training-variables/volume.md)) |
| Enough effort? | Push closer to failure (~1–2 RIR); confirm your RIR calibration ([RPE & RIR](rpe-rir-in-practice.md)) |
| Too much fatigue? | Deload, then resume slightly higher ([Deloads](../05-recovery/deloads-and-rest-days.md)) |
| Recovery inputs? | Sleep, calories, protein adequate? ([Recovery](../05-recovery/index.md), [Nutrition](../04-nutrition/index.md)) |
| Exercise/technique stale? | Swap to an equivalent you can progress; fix execution ([Exercise Selection](../01-training-variables/exercise-selection-and-order.md)) |

Most "plateaus" resolve at the first two rows — insufficient volume or effort — long before you're actually near your genetic ceiling.

## The Evidence

**Progression is the requirement, and logging enables it.** Growth depends on progressively increasing the training stimulus (load, reps, or sets)[^schoenfeld-2017-volume-dose-response] — a log is simply the instrument that makes deliberate progression possible, so it's graded as high-value practice built on strong overload evidence. **[Grade A]** The specific **plateau-troubleshooting order** is a practical framework derived from the volume, effort, and fatigue evidence elsewhere in this Bible rather than a directly-tested protocol, hence the lower grade. **[Grade C]**

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance
- **A single stalled session isn't a plateau** — look for a multi-week pattern before changing anything.
- **True plateaus for advanced lifters** may reflect approaching genetic limits, where progress is inherently slow (see [Genetics](../06-individualization/genetics-and-anthropometry.md)).
- **Over-changing the program** in response to normal fluctuation undermines the progression you're trying to measure.
- **Life factors (sleep, stress, diet)** cause many apparent plateaus and should be ruled out before program changes.
