# What to Track

> **TL;DR** — Track the things you can act on. The most useful metric is your **training performance** — the **load and reps** you do on your key exercises — because progressive overload is the engine of growth and this is the number you steer week to week. Track **bodyweight, tape measurements, and photos** as slower outcome checks, judged over **months**, not days, because they're noisy. Don't confuse the leading indicator (are my lifts progressing?) with the lagging one (am I bigger?).

**Quick recommendations**
- **Log load × reps** on your main exercises every session — this is the actionable metric. **[Grade A]**
- Track **weekly hard sets per muscle** so you know your actual volume (see [Volume](../01-training-variables/volume.md)). **[Grade B]**
- Check **bodyweight (trend), tape measurements, and photos** every few weeks — judge over long windows. **[Grade B]**
- Don't over-read **day-to-day** size/weight changes — they're mostly water, food, and glycogen. **[Grade B]**

## Practical Application

**Two tiers of metrics:**

| Tier | Metrics | How to read them |
|------|---------|------------------|
| Leading (actionable) | Load, reps, RIR, weekly sets | Are my lifts progressing at target effort? Adjust the program from this. |
| Lagging (outcome) | Bodyweight trend, tape measurements, photos | Am I actually growing? Judge over 4–12+ weeks. |

**Steer with performance.** If your load and reps on key lifts are climbing over weeks at a controlled effort (RIR), you're almost certainly building muscle — this is the fastest, most reliable feedback. **Volume** (weekly hard sets) tells you whether you're doing enough work; **RIR** tells you whether it's hard enough.

**Confirm with slow outcome measures.** Bodyweight (as a multi-week *trend*, not a single reading), a few consistent **tape measurements**, and **standardized photos** (same lighting, pose, time of day, unpumped) confirm the physique is changing. These are noisy in the short term, so only trust the direction over months.

## The Evidence

**Progressive overload is the target, so performance is the key metric.** Growth depends on progressively increasing the training stimulus, and volume (hard sets) is its most practical dose measure[^schoenfeld-2017-volume-dose-response] — which is why logging load, reps, and set counts is the highest-value tracking. **[Grade A]** **Size measures are inherently noisy.** Haun and colleagues (2019) detailed how hypertrophy measurements differ by method and are confounded by short-term swelling and fluid shifts[^haun-2019-measurement] — the evidence basis for judging bodyweight/measurements over long windows and steering primarily by performance. **[Grade B]**

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance
- **Strength ≠ size perfectly** — early strength gains are neural, and load can rise faster than muscle; but sustained performance progress is still the best gym-available proxy for growth.
- **Measurement conditions matter** — inconsistent time-of-day, hydration, or "pump" ruin the signal in tape and photos.
- **Bodyweight combines muscle, fat, water, and gut content** — use it as a trend alongside measurements and photos, not alone.
- **What's easy to track (weight) isn't the most informative (performance)** — prioritize the actionable metric.
