# Volume Progression & Deloads

> **TL;DR** — Volume is a resource you spend and must recover from, so program it as a **wave**: start a training block (mesocycle) near your **minimum effective volume**, **add a few sets per muscle per week** as you adapt, and when performance stalls or fatigue piles up, **deload** (pull volume/effort back for ~a week) to recover, then start the next wave a bit higher. This "ramp then unload" cycle lets you use higher volumes than you could sustain continuously.

**Quick recommendations**
- Begin a mesocycle near **MEV** (~8–10 sets for many muscles) and **add ~1–3 sets/muscle/week**. **[Grade C]**
- Push toward your **MRV** (fatigue accumulating, performance plateauing), then **deload**. **[Grade C]**
- **Deload** by cutting volume ~40–60% and/or backing off proximity to failure for ~5–7 days. **[Grade D]**
- Use deloads **proactively** (every ~4–8 weeks) or **reactively** (when recovery/performance tanks). **[Grade D]**

## Practical Application

**The mesocycle wave (typical 4–6 week block):**

| Week | Volume | Effort |
|------|--------|--------|
| 1 | Near MEV (starting sets) | ~2–3 RIR |
| 2–4 | Add ~1–3 sets/muscle each week | Creep toward ~0–1 RIR |
| 5 (peak) | Near MRV | Hard, near failure |
| Deload | ~40–60% of peak volume | Comfortable, ~3–4 RIR |

Then the next block starts slightly higher than the last. Per-muscle landmarks (MEV/MRV) live in the [muscle data files](../../data/muscles/) — remember they're **estimates to adjust from your own response**, not exact numbers.

**Reading the signals to deload:** persistent performance drops across sessions, joints feeling beaten up, sleep/appetite/motivation dipping, or reps falling at the same load. A deload trades a few days of reduced stimulus for dissipated fatigue and protection against overuse — you'll often *feel* fresher afterward, though the one direct trial found no measurable "supercompensation" bounce in strength or size from the week off[^coleman-2024-deload-rct].

## The Evidence

**More volume grows more muscle within a recoverable range** — the reason to progress volume upward at all[^schoenfeld-2017-volume-dose-response], and trained lifters in particular benefit from higher volumes[^schoenfeld-2019-volume-trained-men]. **[Grade A]** But the dose-response has a ceiling set by recovery, and reviews emphasize large individual variation in how much volume is productive[^baz-valle-2022-volume-review]. **[Grade B]** The **wave-and-deload structure** is the practical method for exploiting the "more is better up to your limit" finding without exceeding your recoverable volume. Direct trial evidence has begun to arrive: the first randomized deload trial (Coleman and colleagues, 2024) found a week-long mid-block deload cost **nothing for hypertrophy**, power, or endurance versus training straight through — but produced no extra gains either, and continuous training gained slightly **more lower-body strength**[^coleman-2024-deload-rct]. **[Grade C]** The specific prescriptions (timing, magnitude) remain based on training practice and fatigue-management logic. **[Grade D]**

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance
- **Deload evidence is thin.** That planned deloads improve long-term hypertrophy is plausible and widely practiced but not strongly proven; some lifters autoregulate them reactively instead.
- **MEV/MRV are models, not measurements** — treat the numbers as starting anchors and adjust to your recovery.
- **Not every muscle needs the same wave** — small muscles recover fast; heavily damaged movements (deep RDLs) may need gentler progression.
- **Volume progression isn't infinite** — you reset each block; you don't add sets forever.
