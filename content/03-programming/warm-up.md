# Warm-Ups

> **TL;DR** — Warm up enough to **perform your working sets well and safely**, and no more. A brief **general warm-up** (a few minutes raising your body temperature) plus **specific ramp-up sets** (a few progressively heavier, low-rep sets leading into your first working set) is all most people need. Warm-up sets **don't count toward your hard-set volume** and don't directly add muscle — their job is to prime performance and reduce injury risk on the sets that *do* build muscle. Don't turn warming up into a workout that pre-fatigues you.

**Quick recommendations**
- Do a short **general warm-up** (~3–5 min light cardio / dynamic movement) to start. **[Grade D]**
- Before a heavy exercise, do **2–4 ramp-up sets** of ascending load and descending reps into your first working set. **[Grade C]**
- **Don't count warm-up sets as working volume** — they're sub-failure and don't drive growth. **[Grade B]**
- Warm up **more for heavy compounds**, **less for light isolation**; don't pre-fatigue the target muscle. **[Grade C]**

## Practical Application

**Two layers of warm-up:**

1. **General warm-up** — a few minutes of light cardio or dynamic movement to raise core/muscle temperature and get blood flowing. Enough to break a light sweat; not enough to tire you.
2. **Specific (ramp-up) warm-up** — for each main exercise (especially heavy compounds), do a few sets of ascending load with descending reps to groove the movement and prepare the tissues, e.g. for a working weight of 100 kg × 8: bar × 10, 60 × 5, 80 × 3, 90 × 1, then the working set. Isolation and machine work needs little — often one light feeler set.

**How much is enough?** Warm up in proportion to load and complexity: heavy squats/deadlifts warrant a full ramp-up; a cable curl needs barely any. The goal is readiness, not fatigue — if your warm-up leaves the target muscle tired before its first hard set, you've overdone it and will *lose* working-set performance.

**Stretching note:** brief dynamic movement is fine; long static stretching immediately before heavy lifting can transiently reduce force output, so save extended stretching for after training or separate sessions.

## The Evidence

Warming up is a **performance-and-safety practice, not a growth stimulus.** The muscle-building work is the hard sets taken close to failure; warm-up sets are deliberately sub-failure and therefore **don't add to the volume that drives hypertrophy**[^schoenfeld-2017-volume-dose-response] **[Grade B]** — which is exactly why this Bible counts only *hard* sets. The value of a specific ramp-up is that it lets you perform those hard sets with better technique and full output, and the general injury-risk-reduction rationale is standard coaching practice rather than a strongly-evidenced hypertrophy variable, so it's graded as such. **[Grade C]** In short: warm up to lift well, then let the working sets do the growing.

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance
- **Warm-up's injury-prevention benefit is widely believed but modestly evidenced** in lifting specifically; treat it as prudent practice.
- **Over-warming pre-fatigues** — more is not better; the point is readiness for the working sets.
- **Ramp-up doubles as skill practice** on technical lifts, which is a real (if hard-to-quantify) benefit.
- **Cold, heavy, technical lifts need the most warm-up**; light machine work needs the least.
