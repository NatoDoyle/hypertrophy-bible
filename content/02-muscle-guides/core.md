# Core (Abdominals & Obliques)

> **TL;DR** — The abs are muscles like any other: they grow in response to **progressive resistance**, not endless bodyweight reps. If you want visible, developed abs, **load them** — weighted crunches, cable crunches, and hanging raises — and progress them over time. (Visibility is mostly a **body-fat** question, which is a [nutrition](../04-nutrition/index.md) issue, not a training one.)

**Quick recommendations**
- Train abs with **added resistance** and progress it, like any muscle. **[Grade C]**
- Cover **spinal flexion** (crunch-type) and some **anti-rotation/rotation** for the obliques. **[Grade C]**
- ~**6–16 hard sets/week** is plenty for most; you don't need daily high-rep ab work. **[Grade C]**
- Remember: **abs showing = low body fat**, achieved through diet, not crunches. **[Grade B]**

## Practical Application

| Region | Function | Best picks |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| Rectus abdominis | Spinal flexion | Weighted/cable crunch, hanging leg/knee raise |
| Obliques | Lateral flexion, rotation, anti-rotation | Cable rotations, Pallof press, side bends |
| Deep core (TVA) | Bracing / stabilization | Trained implicitly by heavy compounds |

**Load and progress.** The common mistake is treating abs as an endurance muscle — 100 crunches with no added load. For growth, apply the same rules as elsewhere: enough resistance that ~8–20 reps is hard, taken close to failure, progressed over time. Hanging raises and cable crunches allow easy loading.

**Stabilization vs growth.** Heavy squats, deadlifts, and carries train the deep core's *bracing* function well, but they don't maximally *grow* the rectus abdominis — dedicated, loaded flexion work does that if six-pack development is the goal.

## The Evidence

There is little ab-*specific* hypertrophy research, so the guidance is the **general hypertrophy principles applied to the abs**: growth requires sufficient **mechanical tension and volume with progression**[^schoenfeld-2017-volume-dose-response][^schoenfeld-2010-mechanisms], which for abs means added resistance rather than high-rep bodyweight work. **[Grade C]** Separately, whether abs are *visible* is determined by subcutaneous body fat — a function of energy balance — not by the amount of ab training (and **spot reduction of belly fat is a myth**; see [Myths](../08-myths/index.md)). **[Grade B]**

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance
- **Ab genetics** (number and symmetry of the tendinous intersections that create the "six-pack" blocks) are fixed; training changes size, not the pattern.
- **Anti-movement core training** (planks, Pallof presses) is valuable for function and bracing but is not the most effective way to *grow* the rectus abdominis.
- **You cannot crunch away belly fat** — direct ab work builds the muscle; diet reveals it.
