# Diet Breaks & Refeeds

> **TL;DR** — Long, continuous diets get harder over time — hunger rises, performance drops, and metabolism adapts down. **Diet breaks** (planned ~1–2 week returns to maintenance calories) and **refeeds** (shorter higher-carbohydrate periods) are tools to make dieting more sustainable and may **help preserve muscle and training performance and blunt metabolic adaptation**. They lengthen the total timeline but can improve the *quality* of fat loss and adherence. Keep protein high and keep training hard throughout.

**Quick recommendations**
- On a longer cut, consider a **diet break at maintenance every ~4–8 weeks** (typically 1–2 weeks). **[Grade B]**
- Use **refeeds** (1–2 higher-carb days at ~maintenance) to restore training performance and a mental break. **[Grade C]**
- Keep **protein high and training intensity up** during breaks/refeeds — they're not "cheat" free-for-alls. **[Grade B]**
- Expect a **longer timeline** but potentially better muscle retention and adherence. **[Grade B]**

## Practical Application

**Diet break** = a planned pause where you eat at **maintenance** (not a surplus, not a binge) for ~1–2 weeks. It lets hunger hormones, training performance, and (partly) metabolic rate recover, and gives a psychological reset before resuming the deficit.

**Refeed** = a shorter version — 1–2 days of higher carbohydrate intake at roughly maintenance — used to top up muscle glycogen and restore gym performance during a longer dieting stretch.

**How to program them:**
- On a cut lasting more than ~6–8 weeks, insert a diet break at maintenance; shorter cuts may not need one.
- Keep **protein at cutting levels (~2.0–2.4 g/kg)** and **keep lifting hard** — the point is to relieve the deficit's downsides while still protecting muscle.
- Treat breaks/refeeds as **structured maintenance**, not an excuse to overeat — undoing weeks of deficit in a weekend defeats the purpose.

Trade-off: breaks extend how long the whole cut takes, in exchange for better adherence, performance, and (likely) muscle retention. Whether that trade is worth it depends on your timeline and how you handle continuous dieting.

## The Evidence

**Intermittent dieting improved fat-loss efficiency and blunted metabolic adaptation.** In the MATADOR study, Byrne and colleagues (2018) compared continuous dieting with **intermittent dieting (alternating ~2-week deficit and ~2-week maintenance blocks)** and found the intermittent approach produced **greater fat loss and less metabolic adaptation**, with better weight-loss maintenance afterward[^byrne-2018-matador]. **[Grade B]** (It was done in obese men, so exact translation to lean lifters is uncertain — but it establishes the principle.) **Diet breaks helped performance in athletes.** Peos and colleagues (2021) found that a **1-week diet break during an intermittent dieting regime improved muscle endurance** in adult athletes versus continuous dieting[^peos-2021-diet-break]. **[Grade C]** Together these support diet breaks/refeeds as legitimate tools for the *quality* and sustainability of a cut, layered on top of the fundamentals (moderate deficit, high protein, hard training) covered in [Building Muscle in a Deficit](building-muscle-in-a-deficit.md).

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance
- **Direct muscle-retention evidence is still limited** — the strongest data are on fat-loss efficiency, metabolic adaptation, and performance/adherence, from which muscle-preservation benefits are inferred.
- **Population translation** — MATADOR used obese men; effects in already-lean lifters may be smaller.
- **Discipline required** — a "diet break" only works as *maintenance*; turning it into a surplus erases the deficit.
- **Not always necessary** — short or mild cuts may not need breaks; they matter most for long, aggressive, or repeated dieting.
