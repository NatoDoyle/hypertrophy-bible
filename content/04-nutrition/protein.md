# Protein

> **TL;DR** — Protein is the nutrient that most directly supports building muscle. Aim for roughly **1.6 g of protein per kg of bodyweight per day** (~0.7 g/lb) — more than that adds little for muscle. Spread it across **3–5 meals of ~0.4 g/kg each**, and don't over-worry about exact timing. Total daily protein and total calories do the heavy lifting; the finer points are refinements.

**Quick recommendations**
- Target **~1.6 g/kg/day** (~0.7 g/lb); ~1.6–2.2 g/kg covers essentially everyone with margin. **[Grade A]**
- Distribute across **3–5 meals**, ~**0.4 g/kg per meal** (~20–40 g for most people). **[Grade B]**
- Favor **higher-quality proteins** (rich in leucine/EAAs) — animal sources and blends; plant eaters should eat more total and vary sources. **[Grade B]**
- In a **calorie deficit, go higher** (~2.0–2.4 g/kg) to protect muscle (see [Deficit](building-muscle-in-a-deficit.md)). **[Grade B]**

## Practical Application

**Total daily protein is the priority.** Set it first, then distribute:

| Situation | Protein target |
|-----------|----------------|
| Gaining/maintaining muscle | ~1.6 g/kg/day |
| Practical "plenty" ceiling | ~2.2 g/kg/day (more isn't harmful, just unnecessary) |
| Cutting (deficit) | ~2.0–2.4 g/kg/day |

**Per meal.** Roughly 0.4 g/kg per meal, across 3–5 meals, comfortably maximizes the per-meal muscle-building response for most people. There's no hard "cap" of 20–30 g that the body wastes above — larger doses are still used — but distributing protein is a sensible way to hit both your per-meal and daily targets.

**Quality.** Protein "quality" reflects its essential-amino-acid (especially **leucine**) content and digestibility. Animal proteins (whey, dairy, eggs, meat, fish) are high-quality; most single plant proteins are lower, so plant-based lifters should eat somewhat more total protein and combine sources.

## The Evidence

**~1.6 g/kg/day is the practical ceiling for muscle.** Morton and colleagues' 2018 meta-analysis found that protein intakes **beyond ~1.6 g/kg/day produced no further gains** in resistance-training-induced muscle mass for most people[^morton-2018-protein-meta]. **[Grade A]** Tagawa and colleagues (2020) similarly mapped a **dose-response that plateaus**, with muscle gains rising with protein up to a point and flattening thereafter[^tagawa-2020-protein-dose]. **[Grade A]** An independent 2022 meta-analysis by Nunes and colleagues converges on the same figure — **~1.6 g/kg/day for adults under 65** — while finding **older adults (≥65) benefit at a slightly lower ~1.2–1.6 g/kg/day**[^nunes-2022-protein-meta]. **[Grade A]** Three independent meta-analyses agreeing on ~1.6 g/kg is about as settled as nutrition science gets.

**Distribution: ~0.4 g/kg per meal across several meals.** Schoenfeld and Aragon (2018) reviewed the "how much per meal" question and recommended roughly **0.4 g/kg per meal over at least four meals** to reach ~1.6 g/kg/day and maximally stimulate muscle protein synthesis — while noting the body is not strictly limited to a small fixed per-meal amount[^schoenfeld-2018-protein-per-meal]. **[Grade B]**

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance
- **Higher intakes aren't harmful** — they're just unnecessary for muscle. Some evidence hints at small benefits of higher protein during dieting and for older adults.
- **The "anabolic cap" per meal is a myth** in its strong form; large doses are still used, just with diminishing per-meal efficiency (see [Nutrient Timing](nutrient-timing.md)).
- **Older adults may need more per meal** (~0.4+ g/kg) to overcome "anabolic resistance."
- **Body composition vs bodyweight** — very-high-body-fat individuals may base targets on lean/goal weight to avoid overestimating needs.
