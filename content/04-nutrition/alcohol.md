# Alcohol

> **TL;DR** — Alcohol works against muscle growth. It **acutely blunts muscle protein synthesis** after training, disrupts sleep and recovery, and adds empty calories. You don't have to be a teetotaler to build muscle, but heavy or frequent drinking will slow your progress — especially when consumed around training. Keep it occasional and modest, and avoid it right after hard sessions.

**Quick recommendations**
- Treat alcohol as a **recovery and progress cost** — occasional and modest is fine; frequent/heavy is not. **[Grade B]**
- **Don't drink heavily right after training** — it blunts the post-workout muscle-building response. **[Grade B]**
- Account for its **empty calories** and its **disruption of sleep** (see [Recovery → Sleep](../05-recovery/index.md)). **[Grade C]**

## Practical Application

Alcohol undermines muscle building through several routes at once: it **suppresses the post-exercise rise in muscle protein synthesis**, degrades **sleep quality** (a key recovery input), impairs **judgment around food** (extra calories), and can worsen next-day training. None of this means a drink ends your gains — but the effects are dose-dependent and add up. The practical policy: enjoy alcohol occasionally and in moderation, keep it away from your post-training window, and recognize that a pattern of heavy drinking is one of the easier self-inflicted brakes on progress.

## The Evidence

**Alcohol blunts the muscle-building response to training.** Parr and colleagues (2014) had trained men complete exercise and then consume protein with or without alcohol; **alcohol reduced the maximal post-exercise rates of myofibrillar protein synthesis even when protein was co-ingested**[^parr-2014-alcohol]. **[Grade B]** In other words, drinking after a hard session directly interferes with the signal that builds muscle — protein doesn't fully rescue it. Layered on top are alcohol's well-known effects on sleep and its calorie load, both of which compound the cost over time.

## Key Uncertainties & Nuance
- **Dose and frequency matter most** — the Parr study used a substantial dose; light, infrequent drinking has a smaller impact.
- **The acute MPS blunting** is one session's effect; the long-term hypertrophy consequence depends on how often it's repeated.
- **Indirect effects (sleep, calories, missed/poor sessions)** may matter as much as the direct MPS effect in the real world.
- **Individual tolerance varies**, but no amount of alcohol *helps* muscle growth.
